# Author Platform

## Overview

Your platform is the sum of your visibility and reach as an author — the people who know you, follow you, and will buy your next book because they bought your last one. Platform isn't about being famous; it's about being findable and trusted by the readers who care about what you write. This phase covers building and maintaining your public presence across the channels that matter for your genre.

### Step 2: Choose Your Channels

You cannot be everywhere. Pick 2-3 primary channels and maintain a minimal presence elsewhere.

**Channel selection by genre:**

| Genre | Primary Channels | Secondary Channels |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Literary fiction | Newsletter, Instagram, literary events | Twitter/X, Substack |
| Thriller / Mystery | Newsletter, Instagram, Facebook groups | BookTok, podcasts |
| Romance | BookTok, Instagram, newsletter | Facebook groups, Goodreads |
| Fantasy / Sci-Fi | BookTok, Reddit, newsletter | Twitter/X, Discord, YouTube |
| YA | BookTok, Instagram, YouTube | Twitter/X, Goodreads |
| Nonfiction (general) | Newsletter, LinkedIn, podcast guesting | Twitter/X, YouTube |
| Technical / professional | Newsletter, LinkedIn, conference speaking | YouTube, blog, Twitter/X |

**The one channel every author needs: a newsletter.** You own your subscriber list. Social media platforms change algorithms, lose users, or disappear. Your newsletter is the one channel no platform can take away.

### Step 3: Build Your Newsletter

**Getting started:**
- Choose a platform (Substack, Mailchimp, ConvertKit, Buttondown)
- Create a simple sign-up page linked from your website and social media
- Offer a reason to subscribe (exclusive content, early access, behind-the-scenes)

**Content strategy:**
- Frequency: Monthly or biweekly is sustainable for most authors. Weekly if you have the energy.
- Content mix: Writing updates (30%), personal/behind-the-scenes (30%), recommendations and value (30%), book promotion (10%)
- Tone: Conversational, authentic, like writing to a friend who's interested in your work

**Growth tactics:**
- Link in your social media bios
- Mention in podcast and media appearances
- Include a sign-up prompt in the back matter of your books
- Offer a subscriber-only bonus (short story, deleted scene, early chapter)
- Cross-promote with other authors' newsletters (newsletter swaps)

**Newsletter metrics that matter:**
- Open rate (30-50% is good for author newsletters)
- Click rate (2-5% is typical)
- Subscriber growth rate
- Unsubscribe rate (under 1% per send is healthy)

### Step 4: Build Your Website

Your website is your home base — the one place you control completely.

**Essential pages:**
- Home (who you are, what you write, your latest book)
- Books (all published works with purchase links)
- About (your bio, photo, and story)
- Press/Media (press kit, high-res photos, contact for media inquiries)
- Newsletter sign-up (prominent, not buried)
- Contact (how to reach you or your agent)
- Events (upcoming appearances, if applicable)

**Optional but valuable:**
- Blog (if you write regularly)
- Reading recommendations (builds trust and community)
- For writers (if you teach or mentor)

**Website rules:**
- Keep it simple and fast-loading
- Update it at least quarterly
- Make the newsletter sign-up visible on every page
- Ensure it works well on mobile
- Include purchase links to multiple retailers (not just Amazon)

### Step 5: Social Media Strategy

**General principles:**
- Be a person, not a brand. Readers follow authors they like, not marketing accounts.
- The 80/20 rule: 80% of your content should be interesting, entertaining, or valuable. 20% can be promotional.
- Engage with others more than you broadcast. Comment, share, and participate in conversations.
- Consistency matters more than frequency. Three good posts a week beats seven mediocre ones.

**Content ideas for authors:**
- Writing process insights (without being precious about it)
- Book recommendations (genuine ones — readers trust authors who read widely)
- Behind-the-scenes of publishing (people are curious about how books get made)
- Personal interests that connect to your writing
- Reader interactions (sharing fan art, reader photos, thoughtful reviews)
- Excerpts and teasers (sparingly)
- Other authors' work (generosity builds community)

**What to avoid:**
- Constant "buy my book" posts
- Complaining about reviews, sales, or the publishing industry
- Political or controversial takes (unless that's genuinely part of your brand)
- Engaging with trolls or negative commenters
- Posting when you're angry, tired, or drunk

### Step 6: Speaking and Events

**Types of speaking opportunities:**
- Book festivals and literary events
- Conference panels and keynotes
- Library talks and readings
- University guest lectures
- Podcast and radio interviews
- Workshop and masterclass teaching
- Corporate events (for nonfiction authors)

**How to get speaking invitations:**
- Apply to book festivals (most have open submission periods)
- Pitch yourself to conference organizers
- Build relationships with bookstores and libraries
- Say yes to small events — they lead to bigger ones
- Create a "speaking" page on your website with topics and past appearances
- Ask your publisher's events team for opportunities

**Speaking tips for authors:**
- Prepare, but don't read from a script (unless you're doing a reading)
- Tell stories — audiences remember stories, not information
- Keep readings short (10-15 minutes max)
- Leave time for Q&A — audiences love asking authors questions
- Bring books to sell and sign (coordinate with the venue)
- Collect email addresses for your newsletter

## Techniques

### The Consistency Compound Method
Platform growth is exponential, not linear. Posting consistently for 6 months with modest engagement builds a foundation that accelerates growth in months 7-12. Don't judge your platform by the first 3 months.

### The Value-First Method
Before every post, ask: "Would I find this interesting if I weren't the one who wrote it?" If the answer is no, don't post it. Provide value first; promotion follows naturally.

### The Cross-Pollination Method
Use each platform to drive traffic to the others. Mention your newsletter on social media. Share social media highlights in your newsletter. Reference your blog posts in podcast interviews. Each channel feeds the others.

### The Collaboration Method
Partner with other authors for joint events, newsletter swaps, social media takeovers, and anthology projects. Collaboration exposes you to each other's audiences and is more fun than solo promotion.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A platform audit documenting your current presence
- 2-3 primary channels selected with a content strategy for each
- A newsletter set up and growing
- A professional author website

- A speaking/events strategy
- A 6-month platform growth plan with measurable goals

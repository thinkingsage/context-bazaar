# Agent Evaluation

## Overview

Getting an offer of representation is thrilling — but it's also a business decision. The wrong agent can stall your career as effectively as no agent at all. This phase covers how to evaluate offers, conduct "the call," ask the right questions, and choose the agent who's the best long-term partner for your work.

## Workflow: From Offer to Decision

### Step 1: Receive the Offer

When an agent offers representation:

1. **Thank them warmly** but do not accept on the spot. Say: "Thank you so much — I'm very excited. I'd like to take a couple of weeks to consider and notify other agents who have my materials."
2. **Ask for a timeline.** Most agents expect 2 weeks for a decision. This is standard and professional.
3. **Notify all other agents** who have your query, partial, or full manuscript (see the nudge protocol in query-campaign).
4. **Schedule "the call"** with the offering agent — a 30-60 minute conversation to discuss their vision for your book and career.

### Step 2: Prepare for "The Call"

"The call" is a mutual interview. The agent is evaluating whether you'll be a good client; you're evaluating whether they'll be a good partner.

**Questions to ask the offering agent:**

**About your book:**
- What's your editorial vision for this manuscript? Do you see revisions before submission?
- Which editors and imprints do you see as the best fit?
- How would you position this book in the current market?
- What's your submission strategy — wide submission, targeted, or exclusive?
- What happens if the book doesn't sell on the first round?

**About the business:**
- How do you handle subsidiary rights (foreign, film/TV, audio)?
- Do you work with co-agents for foreign rights, or does the agency handle them in-house?
- What's your commission structure? (Standard: 15% domestic, 20% foreign)
- How do you handle accounting and payments?
- What does your agency agreement look like? Is it per-project or career-wide?
- What's the termination process if either of us wants to part ways?

**About the working relationship:**
- How often do you communicate with clients between deals?
- What's your preferred communication method (email, phone, text)?
- How quickly do you typically respond to client emails?
- How involved are you in your clients' career planning?
- Do you read all your clients' work, or only what you're submitting?

**About your career:**
- How do you see my career developing beyond this book?
- Are you interested in representing me across genres, or primarily in this genre?
- What's your approach to an author's public platform and marketing?
- How do you handle it if a client wants to pivot genres or try something experimental?

**Red flags during the call:**
- Vague answers about submission strategy ("I'll send it to some editors I know")
- Unwillingness to discuss contract terms or commission structure
- Dismissiveness about your questions or career goals
- Pressure to accept immediately
- Negative comments about other agents or clients
- No clear plan for what happens if the book doesn't sell

If you receive multiple offers (congratulations), compare them systematically:

**Decision factors, roughly in order of importance:**

1. **Editorial vision alignment** — Do they understand your book and want to make it better in ways that excite you?
2. **Submission strategy** — Do they have specific editors and imprints in mind? A vague plan is a red flag.
3. **Communication compatibility** — Can you work with this person for years? Trust your instincts here.
4. **Career vision** — Are they thinking beyond this one book?
5. **Track record** — Have they sold books like yours to publishers you respect?
6. **Sub-rights capability** — Strong sub-rights handling can significantly increase your income.
7. **Agency stability** — Is the agency established? What happens if the agent leaves?

### Step 4: Make the Decision

- **Trust your gut, but verify it with data.** If an agent feels right but has no track record, that's a risk. If an agent has a great track record but the call felt off, that's also a risk.
- **Talk to their clients.** Ask the agent if you can speak with 2-3 current clients. If they refuse, that's a red flag. Client references are standard practice.
- **Don't choose based on flattery alone.** The agent who loves your book the most isn't necessarily the best business partner.
- **Consider the agency, not just the agent.** If your agent leaves the agency, what happens to you? Understand the agency's structure.

### Step 5: Accept and Notify

Once you've decided:

1. **Call or email the chosen agent** to accept. Express your enthusiasm.
2. **Email all other agents** to withdraw your manuscript. Be gracious: "Thank you for your time and consideration. I've decided to accept representation elsewhere."
3. **Review and sign the agency agreement.** Read every clause. Ask questions about anything you don't understand.
4. **Celebrate.** Then get ready to work — your agent will likely have revision notes before submission.

## The Agency Agreement

Key terms to understand in the agreement you'll sign:

| Term | What It Means | What to Watch For |
|------|--------------|-------------------|
| Commission rate | Percentage of your earnings the agent takes | 15% domestic, 20% foreign is standard. Higher is unusual. |
| Scope | What the agreement covers | Per-project vs. all work. Understand which. |
| Duration | How long the agreement lasts | Some are open-ended with termination clauses; some have fixed terms. |
| Termination | How either party can end the relationship | 30-60 day written notice is standard. Check what happens to works already on submission. |
| Post-termination | Agent's rights after termination | Agent typically retains commission on deals they made, even after termination. This is fair. |
| Expenses | What costs the agent can pass through | Minor expenses (postage, copies) are normal. Large expenses should require your approval. |

**Get a second opinion.** If anything in the agreement concerns you, consult a publishing attorney or the Authors Guild (if you're a member). This is a business contract — treat it like one.

## Techniques

### The Reference Call Method
When speaking with an agent's current clients, ask: "What surprised you about working with [agent]?" and "If you could change one thing about the relationship, what would it be?" These questions surface honest insights that "Are you happy?" won't.

### The Submission List Test
Ask the offering agent to name 5-8 specific editors they'd submit to. If they can name editors with enthusiasm and reasoning, they have a real plan. If they're vague, they may not have strong relationships in your genre.

### The Career Scenario Method
Describe a hypothetical career scenario to the agent: "What if this book doesn't sell? What if I want to write in a different genre next? What if I get a film inquiry?" Their answers reveal how they think about long-term career management.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- Detailed notes from "the call" with each offering agent
- A comparison matrix if evaluating multiple offers
- Client reference conversations completed
- A signed agency agreement you understand and are comfortable with
- A clear understanding of next steps (typically: revision notes, then submission)

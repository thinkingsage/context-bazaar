# Contract Literacy

## Overview

A publishing contract is a legal document that governs your book's commercial life for years — sometimes decades. Your agent negotiates the terms, but you sign the contract. Understanding what you're agreeing to protects your creative work and financial interests. This phase covers the key clauses, what they mean, and what to watch for.

This is not legal advice. For significant deals, consult a publishing attorney.

## Workflow: Reading and Understanding Your Contract

### Step 1: The Grant of Rights

The most important clause in any publishing contract. It defines what rights you're giving the publisher.

**Rights you may grant:**

| Right | What It Covers | Negotiation Notes |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| Print (hardcover, paperback) | Physical book publication | Core right — always granted |
| E-book | Digital editions | Core right — usually granted |
| Audio | Audiobook production and distribution | Increasingly valuable — consider retaining |
| Foreign/translation | Publication in other languages/territories | Often handled by your agent's sub-rights team |
| Film/TV | Adaptation rights | Almost always retained by the author/agent |
| Serial | Excerpts in magazines/newspapers | Minor right, usually granted |
| Book club | Book club editions | Usually granted |

**Territory:**
- World English rights = English-language publication worldwide
- North American rights = US and Canada only (your agent sells UK/Commonwealth separately)
- World rights = All languages, all territories (publisher handles foreign sales)

**What to negotiate:**
- Retain as many subsidiary rights as possible (especially audio, film/TV, and foreign)
- Your agent's sub-rights team often gets better deals selling these separately
- The more rights you grant, the higher the advance should be

### Step 2: The Option Clause

The option clause gives the publisher the right to consider your next book before you shop it elsewhere.

**Standard option language:**
"The Author grants the Publisher the right of first refusal on the Author's next book-length work."

**What to watch for:**
- **Scope:** "Next book-length work" is broad. Negotiate for "next book-length work in the same genre" or "next book in this series."
- **Matching right vs. first refusal:** "First refusal" means they get to see it first. "Matching right" means they can match any other offer — this is worse for you.
- **Timeline:** The option should have a deadline (30-60 days to respond). Without a deadline, they can sit on your next book indefinitely.
- **Trigger:** The option period should start after publication of the current book, not after delivery. This prevents your next book from being held hostage to publication delays.

**Best case:** No option clause at all. Your agent may be able to negotiate this out entirely.

### Step 3: The Non-Compete Clause

The non-compete restricts you from publishing competing works during the contract term.

**Typical language:**
"The Author agrees not to publish or authorize publication of any work that directly competes with or diminishes the sales of the Work."

**What to watch for:**
- **Breadth:** "Any work" is too broad. Negotiate for "any work on substantially the same subject aimed at the same audience."
- **Duration:** Should be limited to the contract term, not indefinite.
- **Self-publishing:** Ensure the non-compete doesn't prevent you from self-publishing unrelated work.
- **Articles and blog posts:** Ensure the non-compete doesn't restrict your ability to write articles, blog posts, or other short-form content on related topics.

### Step 4: Delivery and Acceptance

This clause defines when you must deliver the manuscript and what "acceptable" means.

**Key terms:**
- **Delivery date:** When the manuscript is due. Build in buffer — late delivery can trigger contract termination.
- **Acceptance:** The publisher must "accept" the manuscript. If they don't, they may have the right to terminate and demand return of the advance.
- **"Satisfactory" vs. "complete":** "Satisfactory in form and content" gives the publisher broad discretion. "Complete" is better for the author.

**What to negotiate:**
- A reasonable delivery timeline with the ability to request extensions
- Clear criteria for what constitutes an acceptable manuscript
- A cure period — if the manuscript isn't accepted, you get a chance to revise before termination
- Advance repayment terms — if the contract is terminated, do you repay the full advance or only a portion?

### Step 5: Reversion of Rights

The reversion clause defines when rights return to you if the book goes out of print or stops selling.

**Why this matters:**
Without a strong reversion clause, a publisher can hold your rights indefinitely — even if they're not actively selling the book.

**Key terms:**
- **Out-of-print definition:** In the digital age, a book is never technically "out of print" if the e-book is available. Negotiate for a sales threshold: "If the Work sells fewer than [X] copies in any consecutive 12-month period, the Author may request reversion."
- **Reversion trigger:** You should have the right to request reversion in writing. The publisher should have 6-12 months to either revert or demonstrate renewed sales effort.
- **What reverts:** All rights granted under the contract should revert, not just print rights.

**Recommended threshold:** 250-500 copies per year is a common negotiating target. Below this, the publisher isn't meaningfully selling your book.

### Step 6: Royalties and Accounting

**Royalty periods:** Typically twice a year (January-June, July-December), with statements arriving 3-4 months after the period ends.

**Reserve against returns:** Publishers withhold a percentage of royalties (typically 25-30%) as a reserve against bookstore returns. This should be released within 2-4 royalty periods.

**Audit rights:** Your contract should include the right to audit the publisher's books. Standard: once per year, at your expense unless discrepancies exceed a threshold (typically 5-10%).

**Joint accounting:** If you have a multi-book deal, check whether the publisher can offset losses on one book against earnings on another. This is called "joint accounting" or "basketing" — it's bad for you. Negotiate for separate accounting per title.

### Step 7: Other Important Clauses

**Copyright:** The copyright should remain in your name. The publisher licenses rights; they don't own your work.

**Cover and title approval:** Negotiate for "meaningful consultation" at minimum. Full approval is rare but worth asking for.

**Editing:** The publisher has the right to edit for grammar and style. Substantive changes should require your approval.

**Warranty and indemnification:** You warrant that the work is original and doesn't infringe on others' rights. The indemnification clause may require you to cover legal costs if someone sues. Negotiate for the publisher to share this burden.

**Assignment:** The publisher may want the right to assign the contract to another publisher (e.g., if they're acquired). Negotiate for your consent to be required for assignment.

## Contract Review Checklist

```markdown
# Contract Review: {Book Title}

## Rights Granted
- [ ] Territory scope is appropriate
- [ ] Subsidiary rights retention reviewed
- [ ] Audio rights: retained / granted (circle one)
- [ ] Film/TV rights: retained / granted
- [ ] Foreign rights: retained / granted

## Financial Terms
- [ ] Advance amount and schedule confirmed
- [ ] Royalty rates match negotiated terms
- [ ] E-book royalty rate is acceptable
- [ ] Reserve against returns is capped and time-limited
- [ ] No joint accounting across titles

## Protective Clauses
- [ ] Option clause is narrow in scope and has a deadline
- [ ] Non-compete is limited to directly competing works
- [ ] Reversion clause has a meaningful sales threshold
- [ ] Delivery terms include a cure period
- [ ] Copyright remains with the author

## Other Terms
- [ ] Cover/title consultation rights included
- [ ] Audit rights included
- [ ] Assignment requires author consent
- [ ] Warranty/indemnification is reasonable
```

## Techniques

### The Clause-by-Clause Method
Read the contract once for overall understanding, then go through clause by clause with a highlighter. Mark anything you don't understand or that concerns you. Discuss every highlighted item with your agent.

### The "What If" Method
For each major clause, ask: "What happens if things go wrong?" What if the book doesn't sell? What if the publisher is acquired? What if I want to leave? What if they want to drop me? The contract should have reasonable answers to all of these.

### The Comparison Method
If you know other authors with similar deals, compare contract terms (with their permission). This helps you understand what's standard and what's negotiable.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A thorough understanding of every clause in your contract
- A completed contract review checklist
- A list of questions or concerns discussed with your agent
- Confidence that the contract protects your interests
- A signed contract (or a clear list of remaining negotiation points)

# Submission to Publishers

## Overview

Once you have an agent, the querying process repeats at a higher level — your agent queries editors at publishing houses on your behalf. This phase demystifies the editorial acquisition process so you understand what's happening, what to expect, and how to be a good partner to your agent during submission.

## Workflow: From Agent to Editor

### Step 1: Pre-Submission Revision

Most agents request revisions before submitting to editors. This is normal and valuable.

**What to expect:**
- An editorial letter outlining suggested changes (structural, character, pacing, prose)
- Timeline of 2-8 weeks for revisions, depending on scope
- Possibly multiple revision rounds before the agent is satisfied

**How to approach it:**
- Take the notes seriously — your agent knows what editors are looking for
- Push back respectfully if you disagree with a specific note, but be open to the overall direction
- Ask clarifying questions rather than guessing at intent
- Deliver on time or communicate early if you need more time

### Step 2: Understand the Submission Strategy

Your agent will develop a submission plan. Ask them to walk you through it:

**Submission approaches:**

| Approach | Description | When Used |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| Wide submission | 10-20 editors simultaneously | High-confidence projects, aiming for auction |
| Targeted submission | 5-8 carefully chosen editors | Most common approach |
| Exclusive submission | One editor at a time | Rare; used when there's a strong existing relationship |
| Tiered submission | Rounds of 5-8, with later rounds informed by earlier feedback | When the agent wants to test the market |

**What your agent prepares:**
- A submission letter (the agent's version of a query letter, tailored to each editor)
- The manuscript (revised and polished)
- Any supporting materials (comp titles, market positioning, author platform info)

**What you should know:**
- Which editors and imprints are on the list
- The submission approach and timeline
- How your agent will communicate updates to you
- What happens if the first round doesn't generate offers

### Step 3: The Waiting Period

Submission to publishers is slower than querying agents. Typical timelines:

| Stage | Timeline |
|-------|----------|
| Editor reads submission | 2-6 weeks |
| Editor shares with colleagues | 1-2 weeks |
| Internal editorial meeting | Scheduled weekly or biweekly |
| Acquisitions/P&L meeting | 1-4 weeks after editorial approval |
| Offer made | Days to weeks after acquisitions approval |

**Total from submission to offer: 4-16 weeks** (if it happens at all).

**During the wait:**
- Work on your next project. Seriously. This is the best use of your time and emotional energy.
- Check in with your agent at reasonable intervals (every 2-3 weeks is fine).
- Don't contact editors directly. Your agent handles all communication.
- Don't obsess over the timeline. Publishing moves slowly.

### Step 4: Understanding Editor Responses

**The pass:**
- Editors pass for many reasons: not right for their list, similar book already acquired, couldn't get internal support, personal taste
- Your agent may share editor feedback (or summarize it). This feedback is valuable for understanding the market.
- A pass is not a judgment on your writing quality. It's a business decision.

**The revision request (R&R):**
- An editor likes the book but wants changes before acquiring
- This is a strong signal of interest — take it seriously
- Discuss with your agent whether the requested changes align with your vision
- If you agree to revise, deliver promptly and professionally

**The offer:**
- An editor wants to acquire your book
- Your agent will notify you and discuss the terms
- If multiple editors are interested, this may trigger an auction (see deal-negotiation)

### Step 5: If the Book Doesn't Sell

Not every book sells on the first round. This is more common than the publishing industry likes to admit.

**Options when a round doesn't generate offers:**

1. **Second round of submissions** — Your agent submits to a new set of editors, possibly with a revised pitch or manuscript adjustments
2. **Revision and resubmission** — If editor feedback points to fixable issues, revise and go out again
3. **Shelve and write the next book** — Sometimes the market isn't ready, or the book needs more development. Writing your next book and selling that first is a legitimate strategy.
4. **Consider independent publishing** — If the traditional path isn't working, self-publishing is a viable alternative (discuss with your agent)

**What not to do:**
- Don't blame your agent without evidence. Submission is hard, and even great agents can't sell every book.
- Don't give up on your career because one book didn't sell. Many successful authors have "drawer novels."
- Don't burn bridges with editors who passed. They may love your next book.

## The Editorial Acquisition Process (What Happens Inside the Publisher)

Understanding the internal process helps you interpret timelines and responses:

1. **Editor reads and loves the manuscript** — This is necessary but not sufficient
2. **Editor shares with colleagues** — Other editors, the editorial director, and sometimes marketing/sales weigh in
3. **Editorial meeting** — The editor formally presents the book to the editorial board
4. **P&L (profit and loss) analysis** — The publisher runs financial projections: estimated sales, production costs, marketing budget, projected profit
5. **Acquisitions meeting** — Senior leadership approves (or declines) the acquisition based on the P&L and editorial enthusiasm
6. **Offer** — If approved, the editor (through your agent) makes an offer

**Why books get rejected internally even when the editor loves them:**
- The P&L doesn't work (projected sales too low for the advance)
- The publisher already has a similar book on their list
- Marketing or sales don't see a clear path to the audience
- The editorial board isn't enthusiastic enough
- Budget constraints for that season's acquisitions

None of these reflect on your writing. They're business decisions.

## Techniques

### The Next Book Method
The single best thing you can do during submission is write your next book. It keeps you productive, reduces anxiety, and gives your agent another project to discuss with editors.

### The Feedback Synthesis Method
If your agent shares editor feedback from passes, look for patterns. If three editors say the pacing lags in the middle, that's actionable intelligence — even if the book sells elsewhere without changes.

### The Long Game Method
Think of submission as one step in a career, not a pass/fail moment. Authors who build careers treat each book as a learning experience and each submission round as market research.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A clear understanding of your agent's submission strategy
- Realistic expectations for timelines and outcomes
- A plan for how you'll spend the waiting period (ideally: writing)
- An understanding of the internal acquisition process
- Emotional preparedness for all possible outcomes

# Scene-by-Scene Drafting

## Overview

This is where the novel gets written. Drafting is the process of turning your outline, characters, and world into actual prose — scene by scene, chapter by chapter. This phase provides a systematic approach to writing your first draft without getting paralyzed by perfectionism.

## Workflow: Writing the Draft

### Step 1: Set Up Your Drafting Practice

Before you write the first word, establish your process:

**Daily word count target:**
- 500 words/day → ~6 months for an 80,000-word novel
- 1,000 words/day → ~3 months
- 2,000 words/day → ~6 weeks (NaNoWriMo pace)

Pick a target you can sustain. Consistency beats intensity.

**Writing sessions:**
- When will you write? (Same time each day builds habit)
- Where will you write? (Consistent environment reduces friction)
- How will you start each session? (Re-read last paragraph, review outline, freewrite)

**First draft mindset:**
- The first draft's only job is to exist
- Give yourself permission to write badly
- Don't edit as you go — that's a different phase
- Forward momentum matters more than quality right now

### Step 2: Scene Planning (Before Each Scene)

Before writing a scene, spend 5 minutes answering:

**Load the right Seed:**
- Check the project manifest's Seed Recommendation Map for this chapter/scene
- If a specific Seed or blend recipe is mapped, load its generation prompt
- If no Seed is mapped, consider the scene's needs: Which POV character? What register (action, interiority, exposition)? What tonal direction?
- The agent should proactively suggest the appropriate Seed based on scene context — see the **Seed Library** phase for the full recommendation workflow

**Purpose:**
- Why does this scene exist? What does it accomplish?
- What changes between the beginning and end of this scene?
- If you removed this scene, would the story still work? (If yes, reconsider whether you need it)

**Scene Elements:**
- **POV character** — Whose perspective are we in?
- **Goal** — What does the POV character want in this scene?
- **Conflict** — What opposes them?
- **Outcome** — Do they get what they want? (Usually: no, or yes but with a complication)
- **Sequel** — How do they react emotionally, and what do they decide to do next?

**Entry and Exit:**
- Enter the scene as late as possible
- Exit as early as possible
- Cut the throat-clearing at the beginning and the lingering at the end

### Step 3: Write the Scene

**Opening the scene:**
- Ground the reader immediately: who, where, when
- Establish the emotional temperature
- Create a question or tension that pulls the reader forward

**The middle:**
- Escalate tension through the scene
- Use dialogue to reveal character and advance conflict
- Balance action, dialogue, and interiority
- Engage the senses — don't just report events, make the reader feel present

**Closing the scene:**
- End on a moment of change, decision, or revelation
- Create a reason to keep reading (hook, question, emotional resonance)
- Don't resolve everything — leave threads for the next scene

### Step 4: Dialogue Craft

Dialogue does multiple jobs simultaneously:

**What good dialogue does:**
- Reveals character (how they speak, what they avoid saying)
- Advances the plot (new information, decisions, confrontations)
- Creates subtext (what's said vs. what's meant)
- Controls pacing (dialogue reads faster than description)

**Dialogue guidelines:**
- Each character should sound distinct — cover the names and see if you can tell who's speaking
- Cut small talk unless it serves a purpose
- Use "said" as the default dialogue tag; it's invisible to readers
- Let action beats replace dialogue tags when possible
- Read dialogue aloud to test naturalness

**Subtext techniques:**
- Characters talk around the real issue
- What they do contradicts what they say
- Silence and deflection carry meaning
- The most important thing in the conversation is never stated directly

### Step 5: Managing the Draft

**Tracking progress:**
- Keep a running word count
- Note which scenes/chapters are drafted
- Mark scenes that need significant revision (but don't revise yet)
- Track continuity notes (character details, timeline, plot threads)
- After drafting each scene, compare the output against the loaded Seed's style profile — if the prose has drifted from the intended voice (sentence length, vocabulary register, emotional distance), note it for revision. This is Seed Diffing applied during drafting rather than waiting for the revision phase.

**When you get stuck:**
- Skip the scene and write the next one you're excited about
- Write the scene badly on purpose — you can fix it later
- Switch to a different character's POV for perspective
- Go back to your outline and re-examine the scene's purpose
- Take a walk — physical movement often unlocks creative blocks

**When to stop for the day:**
- Stop mid-scene if possible (makes it easier to start tomorrow)
- Jot a quick note about what happens next before you close the file
- Don't stop at the end of a chapter — the blank page tomorrow will be harder

## Techniques

### The Scene-Sequel Pattern
Alternate between scenes (action, conflict, outcome) and sequels (reaction, dilemma, decision). This creates a natural rhythm of tension and release.

### The Dialogue-First Draft
Write scenes as pure dialogue first, then layer in action, description, and interiority. This ensures the scene has dramatic momentum before you add texture.

### The Sensory Anchor
Start each scene with one vivid sensory detail that establishes the setting. This grounds the reader without requiring a paragraph of description.

### The Emotional Trajectory
Before writing a scene, note the POV character's emotional state at the start and end. The scene should move them from one to the other.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Info-dumping** — Resist the urge to explain everything. Parcel out information as the reader needs it.
- **Talking heads** — Dialogue without action, setting, or interiority. Ground conversations in physical reality.
- **Flat scenes** — Scenes where nothing changes. Every scene should turn — something must be different at the end.
- **Over-describing** — Trust the reader's imagination. A few well-chosen details are more evocative than exhaustive description.
- **Premature editing** — Polishing sentences in a first draft is procrastination disguised as productivity.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A complete first draft (or substantial progress toward one)
- A scene-by-scene log of what's been written, including which Seed was used for each scene
- Continuity notes for revision
- A list of known issues to address in revision (but not yet fixed)
- Seed drift notes for any scenes where the prose diverged from the intended voice

## Connection to Other Phases

- **Fiction Seeds / Seed Library** — Load the appropriate Seed or blend recipe before each drafting session. The project manifest's Seed Recommendation Map automates this. Update Seed compatibility data (`compatible_with`, `conflicts_with`) based on what you learn during drafting.
- **Revision & Editing** — Seed drift notes from drafting feed directly into the revision process. Use Seed Diffing in Pass 4 to diagnose and correct voice inconsistencies.
- **Series Continuity** — For series writers, ensure you're loading the correct Seed version for the current book's timeline position. Character POV Seeds should match the character's state at this point in the series arc.

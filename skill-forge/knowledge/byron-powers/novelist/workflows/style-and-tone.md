# Style & Tone

## Overview

Style is how you tell the story. Tone is the emotional atmosphere you create. Together, they form the reader's experience of your prose — the difference between a thriller that crackles and one that plods, a literary novel that sings and one that preens. This phase helps you define and maintain a consistent narrative voice throughout your manuscript.

## Workflow: Defining Your Voice

### Step 1: Choose Your Point of View

POV is the most fundamental style decision. Each option shapes what the reader can know and feel:

- **First person** ("I walked into the room") — Intimate, subjective, limited to one consciousness. Good for voice-driven stories, unreliable narrators, and deep interiority.
- **Third person limited** ("She walked into the room") — Flexible, can be close or distant. Good for most fiction. Can shift between characters across chapters.
- **Third person omniscient** ("She walked into the room, unaware that across town...") — God's-eye view. Good for epic scope, ironic distance, or stories with many threads.
- **Second person** ("You walk into the room") — Unusual, immersive, can feel gimmicky if not earned. Good for experimental fiction or choose-your-own-adventure styles.

**Key question:** How close do you want the reader to be to the protagonist's inner experience?

### Step 2: Define Your Prose Style

Consider where your prose falls on these spectrums:

**Sentence Length:**
- Short and punchy → Creates urgency, tension, directness
- Long and flowing → Creates atmosphere, contemplation, immersion
- Mixed → Most effective; varies rhythm to match content

**Vocabulary:**
- Simple and direct → Accessible, fast-paced, clear
- Rich and literary → Atmospheric, layered, slower
- Genre-specific → Establishes authenticity (period language, technical jargon, dialect)

**Density:**
- Sparse (Hemingway-style) → What's left unsaid carries weight
- Lush (Nabokov-style) → Every sentence is crafted for beauty
- Functional (genre-style) → Prose serves story momentum

**Figurative Language:**
- Minimal → Lets action and dialogue carry the weight
- Moderate → Metaphors and similes at key moments for emphasis
- Abundant → Poetic, image-rich, every paragraph paints

### Step 3: Set the Emotional Register

Tone is the emotional coloring of your narrative. Define it along these axes:

**Overall Mood:**
- Dark / Light
- Serious / Playful
- Tense / Relaxed
- Hopeful / Bleak

**Narrative Attitude:**
- Earnest — Takes the story and characters seriously
- Ironic — Maintains distance, sees contradictions
- Wry — Warm but aware of absurdity
- Detached — Observational, clinical

**Humor:**
- None — Straight-faced throughout
- Occasional — Lightens tension at key moments
- Pervasive — Humor is part of the voice even in serious scenes
- Dark — Comedy drawn from grim situations

### Step 4: Establish Pacing Rhythm

Pacing isn't just plot speed — it's prose rhythm:

**Scene-level pacing:**
- Action scenes → Short sentences, active verbs, minimal description
- Emotional scenes → Longer sentences, internal thought, sensory detail
- Transitional scenes → Moderate pace, summary where appropriate

**Chapter-level pacing:**
- How long are your chapters? (Short chapters = faster feel)
- Do chapters end on hooks or resolutions?
- How much time passes between chapters?

**Manuscript-level pacing:**
- Where are the accelerations and decelerations?
- How does pacing shift across the three acts?

### Step 5: Write a Style Sample

Before committing to a full manuscript, write 1,000-2,000 words in your chosen style. Pick a scene from the middle of your story — not the opening, which carries too much pressure.

**Evaluate the sample:**
- Does it feel natural or forced?
- Can you sustain this voice for 80,000+ words?
- Does it serve the story's emotional needs?
- Is it distinct from generic prose?

If it doesn't feel right, adjust and try again. It's much easier to recalibrate now than after 50,000 words.

Once you have a style sample you're happy with, it becomes the source passage for your first Fiction Seed. The **Fiction Seeds** phase walks you through extracting the style profile and writing a generation prompt from this sample. Creating the Seed now — while the style decisions are fresh — means you can reproduce this voice consistently throughout the manuscript.

## Techniques

### The Mentor Text Method
Identify 2-3 published novels whose style resembles what you're aiming for. Analyze their prose: sentence structure, vocabulary, rhythm, tone. Use them as reference points — not to copy, but to calibrate.

### The Read-Aloud Test
Read your prose aloud. Awkward rhythms, clunky phrases, and tonal inconsistencies become obvious when you hear them. If you stumble reading it, the reader will stumble too.

### The Consistency Check
Pick a random passage from your style sample. Does it sound like it belongs in the same book as every other passage? Tonal drift is one of the most common problems in early drafts.

### The Contrast Exercise
Write the same scene in two radically different styles. This helps you understand what your chosen style does well and what it sacrifices.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Purple prose** — Overwriting to sound "literary." If every sentence calls attention to itself, none of them do.
- **Tonal whiplash** — Shifting between comedy and tragedy without transition. Tonal shifts need to be earned.
- **Voice drift** — Starting with a strong voice that gradually flattens into generic prose. Revisit your style sample regularly.
- **Imitation over authenticity** — Copying a favorite author's style instead of developing your own. Influence is fine; mimicry isn't.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A defined POV and narrative distance
- A prose style guide (sentence length, vocabulary, density, figurative language)
- A tonal profile (mood, attitude, humor level)
- A pacing strategy for scenes, chapters, and the full manuscript
- A 1,000-2,000 word style sample that feels right
- 2-3 mentor texts for reference

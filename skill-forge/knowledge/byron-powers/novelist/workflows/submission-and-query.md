# Submission & Query Craft

## Overview

Writing the novel is one skill. Selling it is another. This phase covers the documents and strategies needed to move a finished manuscript from your desk to an agent's inbox and ultimately to a publisher's catalog. Query letters, synopses, pitch documents, and comp titles are distinct crafts from fiction writing — they require compression, precision, and a market-facing mindset that the creative process deliberately avoids.

Most authors dread this phase. The goal here is to make it systematic rather than agonizing.

## The Submission Package

Most literary agents request some combination of these materials. Have all of them ready before you begin querying.

| Document | Length | Purpose |
|----------|--------|---------|
| Query letter | ~250-400 words | Your pitch — hook, stakes, credentials |
| Synopsis | 1-3 pages (single-spaced) | Full plot summary including the ending |
| Sample pages | First 5-50 pages (varies by agent) | Proof that you can write |
| Author bio | 1 short paragraph | Who you are and why you're credible |
| Comp titles | 2-3 titles | Where your book sits in the market |

Some agents also request a one-page pitch, a chapter outline, or a marketing statement. Check each agent's specific submission guidelines — they vary, and following them precisely signals professionalism.

## Part 1: The Query Letter

The query letter is the single most important document in the submission process. It's a 250-400 word business letter that must accomplish three things: hook the agent, convey the story, and establish your credibility.

### Query Letter Structure

**Paragraph 1: The Hook (2-4 sentences)**

Open with the information that makes your book compelling and specific:
- Protagonist (who they are, not just their name)
- Inciting incident (what disrupts their world)
- Stakes (what they stand to lose)

This paragraph should read like jacket copy — vivid, specific, and impossible to put down.

**What works:**
> When forensic accountant Mara Chen discovers her dead father's name in a classified CIA ledger, she has 72 hours to unravel a forty-year conspiracy before the people who silenced him come for her.

**What doesn't work:**
> My novel is about a woman who discovers a secret about her father. It's a thriller with twists and turns that will keep readers on the edge of their seats.

The first is specific and raises questions. The second is generic and tells instead of shows.

**Paragraph 2: The Story (4-8 sentences)**

Expand the hook into a compressed narrative:
- The protagonist's goal and what drives them
- The central conflict and key complications
- The antagonistic force (person, institution, internal struggle)
- The impossible choice or dilemma they face

End this paragraph with a sense of escalation — the reader should feel the stakes tightening. Do not reveal the ending. The query creates a desire to read the manuscript; the synopsis provides the full plot.

**Paragraph 3: The Metadata (2-4 sentences)**

The business information:
- Title, genre, and word count
- Comp titles (see Part 4 below)
- Relevant credentials (previous publications, relevant expertise, awards)
- Why you're querying this specific agent (personalization)

If you have no publishing credentials, don't apologize — just omit that line. The manuscript speaks for itself.

### Query Letter Rules

- **One page maximum.** Agents read hundreds of queries. Respect their time.
- **Third person for the story, first person for the bio.** "When Mara discovers..." not "When my protagonist discovers..."
- **No rhetorical questions.** "What would you do if..." is a cliché in queries.
- **No comparisons to mega-bestsellers.** "The next Gone Girl" makes you sound naive. Use comp titles thoughtfully (see Part 4).
- **No plot summary beyond Act 1 and early Act 2.** The query is a teaser, not a synopsis.
- **No excuses or qualifications.** "This is my first novel but..." undermines confidence. Just pitch the book.
- **Personalize each query.** Mention why you chose this agent — a book they represented, a preference stated on their website, a panel you saw them on.

### Query Letter Revision Process

1. Write the first draft without overthinking — get the story on the page
2. Cut it to 300 words or fewer
3. Read only the first sentence — does it hook?
4. Read only the story paragraph — does it create a desire to read the manuscript?
5. Check: is every sentence specific? Replace any generic language with concrete details
6. Get feedback from other writers (not family or friends who'll be too kind)
7. Revise based on feedback, then let it rest for a week
8. Final polish — read aloud, check for typos, verify word count and genre are accurate

## Part 2: The Synopsis

The synopsis is the document authors hate most. It requires compressing an 80,000-word novel into 1-3 pages while maintaining narrative logic, emotional arc, and — crucially — revealing the ending.

### Synopsis Structure

**Opening paragraph:**
- Introduce the protagonist with a defining detail (not just a name)
- Establish the setting and situation
- Present the inciting incident

**Middle paragraphs (bulk of the synopsis):**
- Follow the main plot thread chronologically
- Hit every major turning point: first plot point, midpoint, crisis, climax
- Include the protagonist's emotional arc — how they change
- Mention key supporting characters only when they affect the main plot
- Include the antagonist's role and motivation

**Final paragraph:**
- The climax — what happens
- The resolution — how it ends
- The protagonist's final state — who they've become

### Synopsis Rules

- **Present tense, third person.** Always. Even if the novel is first person past tense.
- **Reveal the ending.** This is not jacket copy. Agents need to know the full story to evaluate it. Withholding the ending is the most common synopsis mistake.
- **Main plot only.** Subplots get mentioned only if they directly affect the main plot's resolution.
- **Name only 3-5 characters.** Everyone else is described by role ("her sister," "the detective"). Too many names in a synopsis creates confusion.
- **Capitalize character names on first mention.** MARA CHEN discovers... This is industry convention for synopses.
- **No cliffhangers or teases.** "And then everything changes..." is not acceptable in a synopsis. Say what changes.
- **Emotional causality matters.** Don't just list events — show why each event leads to the next and how the protagonist responds.

### Synopsis Length

- **Short synopsis:** 1 page, single-spaced (~500 words). Some agents request this. Brutally compressed — main plot only, no nuance.
- **Standard synopsis:** 2-3 pages, single-spaced (~1,000-1,500 words). Most common request. Room for emotional arc and key complications.
- **Long synopsis:** 5-10 pages. Rare request, usually for non-fiction proposals. Closer to a detailed outline.

Have the short and standard versions ready. You can always cut the standard down to short, but expanding short to standard is harder.

### Synopsis Writing Method

1. Write your novel's plot as a chronological list of 15-20 major events
2. For each event, add the protagonist's emotional response or decision
3. Connect events with causal language ("Because of this, she decides to..." / "This forces him to...")
4. Write it out as flowing prose paragraphs
5. Cut everything that isn't essential to understanding the main plot and the protagonist's arc
6. Read it cold — does it make sense to someone who hasn't read the novel?
7. Check the word count and trim to target length

## Part 3: Sample Pages

Sample pages are the easiest part of the submission package — they're your manuscript. But presentation matters.

### Formatting Standards

- **Standard manuscript format:** 12pt Times New Roman or Courier, double-spaced, 1-inch margins
- **Header:** Author last name / TITLE / page number (right-aligned)
- **First page:** Title and author name centered, then begin the text
- **Scene breaks:** Centered # symbol
- **Chapter breaks:** New page, chapter number/title centered, three blank lines before text begins

### Which Pages to Send

- Always send from the beginning unless specifically asked otherwise
- If the agent requests "first 10 pages," send exactly 10 pages — not 12 because the chapter ends on page 12
- If the agent requests "first three chapters," send the first three chapters regardless of length
- If your opening is slow, fix the opening — don't try to start the sample at a more exciting point

### Opening Page Checklist

Your first page is doing the heaviest lifting in the entire submission:

- Does the first line create a question or establish voice?
- Is the protagonist present (or strongly implied) within the first page?
- Is there a sense of forward momentum — something happening or about to happen?
- Does the voice match the genre expectations?
- Is the prose clean, confident, and free of throat-clearing?

## Part 4: Comp Titles

Comp (comparable) titles tell the agent where your book fits in the market. They're shorthand for audience, tone, and positioning.

### How to Choose Comp Titles

**The formula:** "X meets Y" or "[TITLE] for fans of [TITLE]"

**Rules for good comps:**

- **Published in the last 3-5 years.** Comps older than that signal you're not reading in your genre.
- **Same genre as your book.** Don't comp a literary novel to a thriller, even if they share a theme.
- **Moderately successful.** Not mega-bestsellers (too ambitious) and not obscure (agent won't recognize them). The sweet spot is books that sold well, got reviewed, and are known within the genre.
- **Specific about what you're comping.** "The pacing of [TITLE] with the family dynamics of [TITLE]" is more useful than just naming two books.

**Good comp examples:**
> "TITLE is a domestic thriller in the vein of Megan Abbott's GIVE ME YOUR HAND, with the unreliable narrator tension of RACHEL ABBOTT's THE MURDER GAME."

> "For readers who loved the speculative world-building of PIRANESI and the emotional intimacy of HAMNET."

**Bad comp examples:**
> "It's the next HARRY POTTER." (Too ambitious, too old, wrong signal)
> "There's nothing else like it on the market." (Red flag — means you don't know your market)
> "A mix of Stephen King and Jane Austen." (Incoherent genre positioning)

### Finding Comp Titles

- Browse recent releases in your genre at bookstores and libraries
- Read "best of" lists and award shortlists in your genre from the last 3 years
- Follow agents and editors on social media — they often mention books they loved
- Ask other writers in your genre what they're reading
- Check "readers also bought" sections on bookseller websites

## Part 5: The Submission Process

### Building an Agent List

- Research agents who represent your genre
- Check recent sales in your genre (Publishers Marketplace, agent announcements)
- Read agent interviews and wish lists
- Verify they're currently open to queries
- Aim for a list of 30-50 agents, ranked by fit

### Querying in Batches

Don't send all queries at once. Query in batches of 8-10:

**Batch 1:** Your top 8-10 agents
- Send queries following each agent's specific guidelines
- Wait 4-6 weeks for responses
- Track every query sent, response received, and timeline

**Evaluate Batch 1 results:**
- All rejections, no requests → Revise the query letter before sending more
- Some requests but rejections on the manuscript → The query works but the manuscript needs revision
- No responses at all → Check that queries were received; consider if the genre/concept has market challenges

**Batch 2-5:** Adjust based on feedback and continue

### Tracking Submissions

Maintain a submission log:

```markdown
# Submission Tracker

| Agent | Agency | Date Sent | Materials | Response | Date | Notes |
|-------|--------|-----------|-----------|----------|------|-------|
| {name} | {agency} | {date} | Query + 10pp | Request for full | {date} | Sent full {date} |
| {name} | {agency} | {date} | Query + synopsis | Form rejection | {date} | |
| {name} | {agency} | {date} | Query only | No response | — | Nudge after 8 weeks? |
```

### Handling Responses

- **Request for partial or full manuscript:** Send promptly (within 24-48 hours), formatted per their specifications. Note the date and follow up if you haven't heard back in 8-12 weeks.
- **Personalized rejection:** This is actually encouraging. The agent took time to respond specifically. Note any feedback and consider it seriously.
- **Form rejection:** Don't take it personally. It means the project isn't right for them, not that it's bad.
- **No response:** Many agents operate on a "no response means no" policy. Check their stated response time and move on.
- **Offer of representation:** Notify all other agents who have your materials. Give them 2 weeks to respond. You may end up choosing between offers.

## Techniques

### The Jacket Copy Method
Go to a bookstore and read the back cover copy of 20 books in your genre. Notice the patterns: how they introduce the protagonist, where they cut off the plot summary, what language they use to create urgency. Your query's story paragraph should read like this.

### The Elevator Pitch
Distill your novel to one sentence: "[Protagonist] must [goal] despite [obstacle], or [stakes]." If you can't do this, your premise may not be focused enough — revisit the concept-and-premise phase.

### The Cold Reader Test
Give your query letter to someone who hasn't read your novel. Ask them: "Would you want to read this book?" and "What do you think this book is about?" If their answers don't match your intentions, the query needs revision.

### The Synopsis Reverse-Outline
After writing your synopsis, outline it as if it were a story. Does it have a clear arc? Rising tension? A satisfying resolution? If the synopsis doesn't work as a compressed narrative, it won't convince an agent.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A polished query letter (250-400 words)
- A short synopsis (1 page) and standard synopsis (2-3 pages)
- Properly formatted sample pages
- 2-3 strong comp titles with positioning language
- An author bio paragraph
- A researched agent list of 30-50 names
- A submission tracker ready for use
- A batching strategy for sending queries

## Connection to Other Phases

- **Concept & Premise** — Your premise statement is the seed of your query hook
- **Character Creation** — The protagonist's profile feeds directly into the query's character introduction
- **Plot Structure** — Your outline is the skeleton of your synopsis
- **Style & Tone** — Your sample pages must deliver on the voice your query promises
- **Revision & Editing** — The manuscript must be fully polished before querying begins

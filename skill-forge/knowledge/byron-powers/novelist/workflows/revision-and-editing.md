# Revision & Editing

## Overview

The first draft tells you what the story is. Revision makes it what it should be. This phase provides a systematic, multi-pass approach to transforming a rough draft into a polished manuscript — from big-picture structural work down to line-level prose refinement.

## Workflow: The Revision Passes

### Pass 1: Rest and Re-Read

**Before you change anything, create distance.**

- Put the manuscript away for at least 2-4 weeks (longer if you can)
- Work on something else — a short story, reading, or planning your next project
- When you return, read the entire manuscript in as few sittings as possible
- Read as a reader, not a writer — note your reactions, not your fixes
- Mark passages where your attention wanders, where you're confused, or where you're genuinely engaged

**After the read-through, write a letter to yourself:**
- What works? What are the strengths?
- What doesn't work? Where did you lose interest?
- What surprised you? What did you forget you'd written?
- What's the biggest single problem with the manuscript?

### Pass 2: Structural Revision

Address the big-picture issues first. There's no point polishing prose in a scene you might cut.

**Story structure:**
- Does the inciting incident happen early enough?
- Does the middle sag? Where does momentum stall?
- Does the climax deliver on the story's promise?
- Is the ending earned?

**Character arcs:**
- Does the protagonist change believably?
- Is the antagonist's motivation clear and compelling?
- Do supporting characters serve the story?
- Are there characters who should be combined or cut?

**Pacing:**
- Are there scenes that can be cut without losing anything essential?
- Are there gaps where scenes need to be added?
- Does the pacing accelerate toward the climax?
- Are quiet moments earned and purposeful?

**Plot logic:**
- Are there plot holes or contradictions?
- Does cause-and-effect hold throughout?
- Are coincidences minimized (especially in the resolution)?
- Is information revealed at the right time?

**Actions in this pass:** Cut scenes, add scenes, reorder chapters, rewrite turning points, restructure acts. This is major surgery.

### Pass 3: Scene-Level Revision

With the structure solid, refine each scene:

**For each scene, ask:**
- Does this scene have a clear purpose? (If not, cut or combine it)
- Does something change by the end of the scene?
- Does the scene start late enough and end early enough?
- Is the conflict clear and escalating?
- Is the POV consistent and the right choice for this scene?

**Dialogue check:**
- Does each character sound distinct?
- Is there subtext, or is everything on the nose?
- Can dialogue tags be reduced or replaced with action beats?
- Is there unnecessary small talk or exposition disguised as conversation?

**Description and setting:**
- Is the setting grounded in sensory detail?
- Is description woven into action, or dumped in blocks?
- Are you showing the world through the POV character's perception?

### Pass 4: Line Editing

Now focus on prose quality, paragraph by paragraph:

**Clarity:**
- Is every sentence clear on first reading?
- Are there ambiguous pronouns or confusing constructions?
- Can complex sentences be simplified without losing meaning?

**Economy:**
- Cut redundant words and phrases
- Eliminate filler (just, really, very, quite, somewhat, rather)
- Remove unnecessary adverbs — if the verb is strong enough, it doesn't need modification
- Tighten wordy constructions ("the fact that" → cut; "in order to" → "to")

**Rhythm and flow:**
- Vary sentence length — monotonous rhythm puts readers to sleep
- Read passages aloud to catch awkward phrasing
- Check paragraph transitions — does each paragraph flow naturally to the next?
- Ensure the prose rhythm matches the emotional content

**Voice consistency:**
- Does the narrative voice stay consistent throughout?
- Are there passages where the style shifts unintentionally?
- Does the voice match the tone established in your style guide?
- **Seed Diffing:** For each chapter or scene, compare the prose against its intended Seed's style profile. Check sentence length, vocabulary register, figurative language density, and emotional distance against the Seed's parameters. If the prose has drifted, the Seed's generation prompt tells you exactly what to correct toward. Update the Seed's `drift_notes` in its frontmatter if you discover consistent patterns — this feeds back into the Seed Library's audit process.

### Pass 5: Continuity and Polish

The final pass catches the small things:

**Continuity:**
- Character names, appearances, and details consistent throughout
- Timeline makes sense (days of the week, seasons, travel time)
- Objects and settings remain consistent between scenes
- Information characters know matches what they've been told or witnessed

**Technical polish:**
- Spelling and grammar
- Punctuation consistency (serial comma or not, dash usage)
- Formatting consistency (chapter headings, scene breaks)
- Word repetition (search for overused words and vary them)

**Opening and closing:**
- Does the first page hook the reader?
- Does the first chapter establish voice, character, and a reason to keep reading?
- Does the final chapter deliver emotional satisfaction?
- Does the last line resonate?

## Techniques

### The Reverse Read
Read the manuscript backward, chapter by chapter. This breaks your familiarity with the flow and helps you evaluate each chapter on its own merits.

### The Highlight Method
Print the manuscript (or use digital highlighting) and color-code: action (one color), dialogue (another), description (another), interiority (another). This reveals imbalances — too much of one element, not enough of another.

### The Kill Your Darlings Audit
Identify your five favorite passages. Ask honestly: do they serve the story, or do you love them for their own sake? If they don't serve the story, cut them. Save them in a separate file if it helps.

### The Beta Reader Protocol
After your own revision passes, get outside readers:
- Choose readers who represent your target audience
- Give them specific questions, not just "what do you think?"
- Look for patterns across multiple readers — if three people flag the same issue, it's real
- You don't have to take every suggestion, but you should understand every criticism

## Common Pitfalls

- **Revising too soon** — Editing before the draft is complete often leads to endless polishing of early chapters while the ending never gets written.
- **Revising in the wrong order** — Line editing before structural revision wastes time on prose that might get cut.
- **Over-revising** — At some point, revision becomes avoidance. The manuscript will never be perfect. It needs to be done.
- **Ignoring feedback patterns** — If multiple readers flag the same problem, the problem is real even if you disagree with their proposed solutions.
- **Losing the voice** — Over-editing can sand away the personality and energy of the prose. Keep some rough edges if they serve the voice.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A structurally sound manuscript
- Consistent, well-developed characters throughout
- Polished prose with a consistent voice
- Clean continuity with no contradictions
- A manuscript ready for beta readers or submission

## What Comes Next

The Novelist power covers the creative process from concept to polished manuscript. Beyond this point, the path depends on your goals:
- **Traditional publishing** — Query letters, agent research, submission
- **Self-publishing** — Professional editing, cover design, formatting, distribution
- **Personal satisfaction** — You wrote a novel. That's a real accomplishment.

Whatever your path, the hard part is done. You made something from nothing.

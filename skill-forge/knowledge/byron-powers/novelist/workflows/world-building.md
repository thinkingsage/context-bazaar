# World-Building & Setting

## Overview

Setting is more than backdrop — it shapes character behavior, constrains plot possibilities, and creates atmosphere. This phase builds your story's world with the right level of detail: enough to feel real, not so much that it overwhelms the narrative.

## Workflow: Constructing Your World

### Step 1: Determine the Scope

How much world-building does your story actually need?

- **Contemporary realistic fiction** — Minimal. Focus on specific locations, social dynamics, and atmosphere.
- **Historical fiction** — Moderate. Research the period, but only detail what affects your characters.
- **Fantasy/Sci-fi** — Extensive. You're building from scratch, but still only detail what the story touches.

**The iceberg principle:** Know ten times more about your world than you put on the page. The depth shows through even when it's not explicit.

### Step 2: Build the Physical World

Start with the tangible environment:

**Geography & Climate:**
- Where does the story take place? (city, rural, multiple locations)
- What does it look, sound, smell, and feel like?
- How does the environment affect daily life?
- What are the seasons, weather patterns, natural hazards?

**Built Environment:**
- Architecture, infrastructure, technology level
- Key locations where scenes will take place
- How people move around (transportation, distances)
- What the spaces say about the people who built them

**Sensory Palette:**
- What are the dominant sensory experiences of this world?
- What does this place smell like? Sound like at night?
- What textures and temperatures define it?

### Step 3: Build the Social World

People don't exist in a vacuum. Define the social structures:

**Power & Hierarchy:**
- Who has power and how did they get it?
- What institutions govern daily life?
- What are the social classes or groups?
- How is conflict resolved (law, custom, force)?

**Culture & Custom:**
- What do people believe? (religion, philosophy, superstition)
- What are the social norms and taboos?
- How do people mark important life events?
- What do they value most?

**Economy & Resources:**
- How do people make a living?
- What's scarce? What's abundant?
- How does money (or its equivalent) flow?
- What economic pressures affect your characters?

### Step 4: Establish the Rules

Every world has rules — even realistic ones. Define what's possible:

- **For speculative fiction:** What are the rules of magic, technology, or the supernatural? What are the costs and limitations?
- **For realistic fiction:** What are the social rules, legal constraints, and practical limitations your characters face?
- **For all fiction:** What are the unwritten rules of your story's world that characters know instinctively?

Consistency matters more than complexity. Readers will accept almost any rule as long as you follow it.

### Step 5: Connect World to Story

The world should serve the narrative, not the other way around:

- How does the setting create or intensify the central conflict?
- How does the world constrain your characters' choices?
- What aspects of the world reflect or contrast with the theme?
- What details of the world will readers need to understand the plot?

If a world-building detail doesn't affect character, plot, or theme, it might not belong in the manuscript — even if it's interesting.

## Techniques

### The Day-in-the-Life Method
Write a typical day for an ordinary person in your world (not your protagonist). This reveals the texture of daily life and often surfaces details you hadn't considered.

### The Five Senses Walk
Describe a walk through a key location using all five senses. This grounds the setting in physical reality and gives you material for scene-setting.

### The History Method
Write a brief timeline of your world's recent history (last 50-100 years). Understanding how the world got to its current state helps you write it with authority.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A world bible or setting document covering physical and social elements
- Key locations described with sensory detail
- Rules and constraints that affect the plot
- Clear connections between setting and story
- Enough depth to write with confidence, without over-building

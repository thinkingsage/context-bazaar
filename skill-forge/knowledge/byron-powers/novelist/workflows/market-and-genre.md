# Market & Genre Research

## Overview

Understanding your genre isn't about writing to formula — it's about knowing the conversation your book is entering. Every novel exists within a market context: reader expectations, genre conventions, current trends, and competitive titles. This phase helps you research that context so you can position your work intelligently, whether you're writing to satisfy genre expectations or deliberately subverting them.

Market awareness doesn't replace artistic vision. It informs it. The authors who succeed commercially and critically tend to know their genre deeply enough to work within it with confidence and originality.

## When to Use This Phase

This phase is useful at two points in the process:

- **Before drafting** — To understand the landscape you're writing into, calibrate word count and pacing expectations, and identify opportunities for originality within your genre
- **Before submission** — To select comp titles, position your manuscript, and write a market-aware query letter

You can also return to it mid-draft if you're uncertain whether your story is meeting, exceeding, or breaking genre expectations — and whether that's intentional.

## Part 1: Genre Identification

### Step 1: Name Your Genre and Subgenre

Publishing genres are more specific than bookstore shelf categories. Identify where your novel fits:

**Primary genre** — The broadest category:
- Literary Fiction
- Mystery / Crime
- Thriller / Suspense
- Science Fiction
- Fantasy
- Romance
- Horror
- Historical Fiction
- Young Adult
- Middle Grade

**Subgenre** — The more specific niche:

| Primary Genre | Common Subgenres |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Mystery | Cozy, police procedural, amateur sleuth, noir, locked room, historical mystery |
| Thriller | Psychological, domestic, legal, medical, political, espionage, techno-thriller |
| Science Fiction | Hard SF, space opera, cyberpunk, climate fiction, near-future, first contact |
| Fantasy | Epic/high, urban, dark, portal, romantasy, mythic, low fantasy, grimdark |
| Romance | Contemporary, historical, paranormal, romantic suspense, rom-com |
| Horror | Psychological, supernatural, cosmic, gothic, slasher, folk horror, body horror |
| Literary Fiction | Domestic, autofiction, speculative literary, historical literary, experimental |

**Cross-genre** — If your novel straddles categories:
- Identify the primary genre (where it would be shelved)
- Identify the secondary genre (the flavor it borrows)
- Example: "Literary thriller" = literary fiction primary, thriller pacing and structure
- Example: "Romantasy" = fantasy primary, romance subplot structure

### Step 2: Verify Your Classification

Test your genre identification:
- If you walked into a bookstore, which section would your book be in?
- If a reader who loves your genre picked up your book, would they feel at home?
- If a reader who dislikes your genre picked it up, would they put it down? (This is actually a good sign — it means you're delivering on genre promises)

If you can't answer these questions clearly, your genre identity may be muddled. That's a positioning problem worth solving before you query.

## Part 2: Genre Conventions

Every genre has conventions — structural patterns, character types, pacing expectations, and narrative promises that readers expect. Knowing them lets you fulfill, exceed, or subvert them intentionally.

### Step 1: Map the Core Conventions

For your genre and subgenre, research and document:

**Structural conventions:**
- Expected word count range
- Typical pacing pattern (fast start? slow burn? escalating?)
- Common structural frameworks (three-act, dual timeline, etc.)
- Chapter length norms
- Ending expectations (resolved? ambiguous? cliffhanger for series?)

**Word count guidelines by genre:**

| Genre | Typical Range | Notes |
|-------|--------------|-------|
| Literary Fiction | 70,000 - 100,000 | Shorter is trending |
| Mystery / Crime | 70,000 - 90,000 | Cozies can be shorter (60-75K) |
| Thriller | 80,000 - 100,000 | Pacing matters more than length |
| Science Fiction | 80,000 - 120,000 | Hard SF and space opera can run longer |
| Fantasy | 90,000 - 130,000 | Epic fantasy tolerates 150K+; urban fantasy runs shorter |
| Romance | 50,000 - 90,000 | Category romance: 50-70K; single title: 80-100K |
| Horror | 70,000 - 90,000 | Shorter is often more effective |
| Historical Fiction | 80,000 - 110,000 | Research depth can justify length |
| Young Adult | 55,000 - 80,000 | Fantasy YA can push to 100K |
| Middle Grade | 25,000 - 55,000 | Depends heavily on age target |

**Character conventions:**
- Expected protagonist types (detective in mystery, chosen one in fantasy, etc.)
- Antagonist patterns (human villain, systemic force, internal conflict)
- Relationship dynamics readers expect (romance arc, mentor figure, found family)
- Character arc patterns (redemption, coming-of-age, disillusionment)

**Content conventions:**
- Violence level (graphic, implied, off-page)
- Romance/intimacy level (closed door, fade to black, explicit)
- Language register (literary, accessible, genre-specific vocabulary)
- Darkness level (how bleak can it get? does the genre demand hope?)

**Narrative promises:**
Every genre makes implicit promises to the reader:
- Mystery promises: the crime will be solved
- Romance promises: the couple will end up together (HEA or HFN)
- Thriller promises: the stakes will escalate and the tension will pay off
- Horror promises: the reader will feel fear
- Literary fiction promises: the prose and insight will reward close attention

Breaking these promises is possible but risky. Know what you're breaking and why.

### Step 2: Document Your Compliance and Departures

Create a genre compliance map for your manuscript:

```markdown
# Genre Compliance Map: {Title}

Genre: {primary} / {subgenre}

## Conventions Met
- {Convention}: {How your manuscript fulfills it}
- {Convention}: {How your manuscript fulfills it}

## Conventions Exceeded
- {Convention}: {How your manuscript goes beyond the expectation}

## Conventions Subverted
- {Convention}: {How and why you're breaking it}
- Risk level: {low/medium/high}
- Mitigation: {What you're offering instead}

## Conventions Ignored
- {Convention}: {Why it's absent and whether that matters}
```

The goal isn't 100% compliance — it's awareness. The most interesting genre fiction often subverts one or two conventions while honoring the rest. But subversion only works when the author clearly knows the convention they're breaking.

## Part 3: Competitive Landscape

### Step 1: Build a Reading List

Read widely and recently in your genre. Aim for 10-15 titles published in the last 3 years:

**Selection criteria:**
- 3-4 bestsellers (what's commercially dominant right now)
- 3-4 critically acclaimed titles (what's getting awards and review attention)
- 3-4 debut novels (what agents and editors are currently acquiring)
- 2-3 titles that feel closest to your own book (your direct competition)

### Step 2: Analyze Each Title

For each book you read, note:

```markdown
# Comp Analysis: {Title} by {Author}

Published: {year}
Publisher: {publisher}
Word count: {approximate}
Subgenre: {subgenre}

## What it does well
- {Strength 1}
- {Strength 2}

## Market positioning
- {How it was marketed — cover style, jacket copy tone, shelf placement}

## Relevance to my book
- {What my book shares with this one}
- {How my book differs}
- {Could this be a comp title? Why or why not?}
```

### Step 3: Identify the Gap

After analyzing the competitive landscape, articulate what your book offers that isn't already on the shelf:

- What combination of elements is unique to your manuscript?
- What reader desire is underserved in the current market?
- What fresh perspective or voice are you bringing?

This isn't about being completely unprecedented — it's about identifying your specific contribution to the ongoing conversation in your genre.

## Part 4: Trend Awareness

### Current vs. Enduring

Distinguish between:

- **Trends** — Temporary surges in popularity (unreliable narrators, dark academia, cozy fantasy). These cycle in and out over 2-5 years.
- **Enduring patterns** — Persistent reader preferences that outlast any single trend (strong voice, emotional stakes, satisfying endings).

**How to use trend awareness:**
- Don't chase trends — by the time you finish a novel, the trend may have passed
- Don't ignore trends — if your book naturally aligns with a current trend, lean into it in your positioning
- Do write toward enduring patterns — these are always marketable
- Do be aware of trend fatigue — if the market is saturated with a particular type of book, yours needs to stand out more sharply

### Tracking Trends

**Sources for market intelligence:**
- Publisher's Marketplace deal reports (what's being acquired now)
- Agent wish lists and interviews (what they're looking for)
- Award shortlists and longlists (what's getting critical attention)
- Bestseller lists by genre (what's selling)
- Bookstagram, BookTok, and book review communities (what readers are excited about)
- Trade publications (Publishers Weekly, The Bookseller, Kirkus)

### Trend Analysis Template

```markdown
# Genre Trend Analysis: {Genre}

Date: {date}

## Currently Hot
- {Trend 1}: {Description, examples, estimated saturation level}
- {Trend 2}: {Description, examples, estimated saturation level}

## Emerging
- {Trend 1}: {Early signals, debut titles leading the way}
- {Trend 2}: {Early signals}

## Declining
- {Trend 1}: {Was hot 2-3 years ago, market is saturated}
- {Trend 2}: {Reader fatigue setting in}

## Enduring
- {Pattern 1}: {Always works, always will}
- {Pattern 2}: {Consistent reader demand}

## My Book's Position
- Aligns with: {trends or enduring patterns}
- Differentiates from: {saturated trends}
- Timing assessment: {good/neutral/challenging}
```

## Part 5: Positioning Statement

### Crafting Your Position

Synthesize your research into a clear positioning statement — one paragraph that captures where your book sits in the market:

**Template:**
"{TITLE} is a {word count} {genre/subgenre} that will appeal to readers of {comp 1} and {comp 2}. It combines {element from comp 1} with {element from comp 2}, while offering {your unique contribution}. Set in {setting/context}, it explores {theme} through {brief premise hook}."

**Example:**
"THE LIAR'S GARDEN is an 85,000-word domestic thriller that will appeal to readers of Megan Abbott and Tana French. It combines Abbott's razor-sharp dissection of female ambition with French's atmospheric sense of place, while offering a multi-generational structure that traces a family secret across three decades. Set in a decaying coastal town in Maine, it explores how the stories families tell themselves become indistinguishable from the lies they hide."

### Positioning Pitfalls

- **Too broad:** "A literary novel about life and love." This describes thousands of books.
- **Too narrow:** "A second-person present-tense novel about a left-handed ceramicist in 1970s Lisbon." Specificity is good; obscurity isn't.
- **Contradictory signals:** "A fast-paced literary meditation." Pick a lane or explain the hybrid clearly.
- **Trend-dependent:** "The next [hot trend] novel." If the trend fades, your positioning collapses.

## Techniques

### The Bookstore Walk
Spend an hour in the section where your book would be shelved. Read jacket copy, note cover design trends, see what's face-out (promoted), and observe what other customers pick up. This is market research you can't get online.

### The Review Mining Method
Read 20-30 reader reviews (not professional reviews) of your closest comp titles. Pay attention to what readers praise and complain about. Their language reveals what the genre's audience values and what frustrates them.

### The Agent Wishlist Scan
Read 20-30 agent wishlists in your genre. Note recurring requests — these signal market gaps. If multiple agents are asking for the same thing and your book delivers it, that's a strong positioning signal.

### The Negative Space Method
List the 10 most common books in your subgenre. Then ask: what's missing? What perspective, setting, character type, or structural approach is absent? Your book's originality may live in that negative space.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A clear genre and subgenre classification
- A genre compliance map documenting conventions met, exceeded, and subverted
- A competitive landscape analysis with 10-15 recent titles
- A trend analysis for your genre
- 2-3 validated comp titles with positioning language
- A positioning statement
- A complete genre research document for reference throughout submission

## Connection to Other Phases

- **Concept & Premise** — Genre awareness can sharpen your premise by clarifying what's fresh about it
- **Plot Structure** — Genre conventions inform structural expectations (mystery needs a solution, romance needs an HEA)
- **Style & Tone** — Genre expectations affect prose register and pacing
- **Manuscript Diagnostics** — Structural balance analysis can check against genre word count and pacing norms
- **Submission & Query** — Comp titles, positioning statement, and genre classification feed directly into the query letter and synopsis

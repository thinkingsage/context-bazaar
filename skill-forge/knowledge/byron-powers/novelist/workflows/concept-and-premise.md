# Concept & Premise

## Overview

Every novel starts with an idea, but an idea alone isn't enough. This phase transforms a raw concept into a working premise — a clear statement of what your story is about, who it happens to, what's at stake, and why it matters.

## Workflow: From Idea to Premise

### Step 1: Capture the Seed

Start with whatever you have — a character, a situation, an image, a question, a feeling. Write it down without judgment. Don't worry about whether it's "good enough."

**Prompts to explore:**
- What image or moment won't leave your head?
- What question do you want to explore through fiction?
- What kind of emotional experience do you want to give the reader?
- Is there a real-world issue or theme you want to examine through story?

### Step 2: Find the Conflict

A premise needs tension. Identify the central conflict by asking:

- **Who wants something?** (protagonist)
- **What do they want?** (goal/desire)
- **Why can't they have it?** (obstacle)
- **What happens if they fail?** (stakes)

The stronger the opposition between desire and obstacle, the more compelling the premise.

### Step 3: Identify the Theme

Theme is the underlying argument or question your novel explores. It doesn't need to be a message — it can be a tension between two ideas.

**Examples:**
- Freedom vs. security
- The cost of ambition
- Whether people can truly change
- The nature of justice

Your theme should connect naturally to your conflict. If your protagonist wants freedom but is trapped by obligation, your theme might explore duty vs. self-determination.

### Step 4: Write the Premise Statement

Combine your elements into a one-to-three sentence premise statement:

**Template:**
"When [inciting incident], [protagonist] must [goal] despite [obstacle], or else [stakes]. Along the way, they confront [theme]."

**Example:**
"When a retired detective's daughter goes missing, he must navigate the criminal underworld he left behind despite the enemies he made there, or lose the only person he has left. Along the way, he confronts whether the justice he once served was ever real."

### Step 5: Stress-Test the Premise

Ask these questions:
- Does this sustain a full novel, or is it a short story idea?
- Is the protagonist active (making choices) or passive (things happening to them)?
- Are the stakes personal enough to care about?
- Is there room for the protagonist to change?
- Does the conflict escalate naturally, or is it one-note?

If the premise feels thin, revisit Steps 2-4 and deepen the conflict or stakes.

## Techniques

### The "What If" Method
Start with a speculative question: "What if [unusual situation]?" Then ground it with a specific character and stakes.

### The Intersection Method
Combine two unrelated ideas, genres, or settings. The friction between them often produces original premises.

### The Personal Obsession Method
Write about what genuinely fascinates or disturbs you. Authentic curiosity produces the most compelling fiction.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A clear premise statement (1-3 sentences)
- An identified protagonist with a goal
- A central conflict with meaningful stakes
- A working theme or thematic question
- Confidence that the idea can sustain a novel-length work

## Next Phase

Once your premise is solid, move to **character-creation** to build the cast who will bring this story to life.

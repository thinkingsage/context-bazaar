# Character Creation

## Overview

Characters are the engine of your novel. Readers follow stories because they care about people — even fictional ones. This phase builds multi-dimensional characters with histories, desires, flaws, and voices that feel real on the page.

## Workflow: Building Your Cast

### Step 1: Identify Your Core Cast

Start with the essential roles your story needs:

- **Protagonist** — Whose story is this? Who changes the most?
- **Antagonist** — Who or what opposes the protagonist? (Can be a person, institution, force of nature, or internal conflict)
- **Key supporting characters** — Who else is essential to the plot? (mentor, ally, love interest, rival)

Keep the core cast small. A focused cast is easier to develop deeply and less confusing for readers.

### Step 2: Build Character Profiles

For each major character, work through these layers:

**Surface Layer (what the world sees):**
- Name, age, appearance
- Occupation, social role
- Mannerisms, habits, speech patterns
- First impression they give others

**Psychological Layer (what drives them):**
- Core desire — what do they want more than anything?
- Core fear — what are they most afraid of?
- Misbelief — what false belief shapes their worldview?
- Moral code — what lines will they not cross? (And what would make them cross those lines?)

**Backstory Layer (what shaped them):**
- Formative experiences that created their desires and fears
- Key relationships that influenced who they are
- Wounds they carry — emotional, physical, or both
- What they were like before the story begins

**Arc Layer (how they change):**
- Where do they start emotionally/psychologically?
- What do they need to learn or accept?
- What forces them to change?
- Where do they end up?

### Step 3: Create Character Relationships

Map how your characters connect to and affect each other:

- What does each character want from the others?
- Where do their goals align? Where do they clash?
- What secrets do they keep from each other?
- How does each relationship create tension or support?

The most interesting relationships involve characters who need each other but also frustrate each other.

### Step 4: Find Each Character's Voice

Each character should sound distinct in dialogue and internal thought. Consider:

- **Vocabulary** — Educated or colloquial? Precise or vague? Formal or casual?
- **Rhythm** — Short, punchy sentences or long, flowing ones?
- **Attitude** — Optimistic, cynical, guarded, blunt, evasive?
- **Verbal tics** — Repeated phrases, filler words, characteristic expressions?

Write a short monologue from each major character's perspective to test their voice.

**Creating Character POV Seeds:**
If your novel uses multiple POV characters, each one's narrative voice should be captured as a Fiction Seed. A character POV Seed defines not just how the character speaks (that's dialogue) but how the narrative sounds when filtered through their perception — what they notice, how they process information, the rhythm of their thoughts. Use the monologue you just wrote as the sample passage for the Seed. See the **Fiction Seeds** phase for the Seed creation workflow and the **Seed Library** phase for organizing character Seeds within your project manifest.

### Step 5: Validate Against the Premise

Check that your characters serve the story:

- Does the protagonist's desire connect to the central conflict?
- Does the antagonist's goal create genuine opposition?
- Does each supporting character have a reason to be in the story?
- Do the character arcs connect to the theme?

Cut or combine characters who don't earn their place.

## Techniques

### The Interview Method
Write a scene where you interview your character. Ask them unexpected questions and let them answer in their own voice. You'll discover things you didn't plan.

### The Contradiction Method
Give each character at least one internal contradiction — a brave person who's afraid of intimacy, a generous person who hoards secrets. Contradictions create depth.

### The Want vs. Need Method
Separate what the character wants (conscious goal) from what they need (unconscious growth). The tension between want and need drives the arc.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- Character profiles for all major characters
- A relationship map showing connections and tensions
- Distinct voices for each character
- Clear character arcs that connect to the theme
- Confidence that your cast can carry the story

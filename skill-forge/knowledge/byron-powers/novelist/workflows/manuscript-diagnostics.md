# Manuscript Diagnostics

## Overview

Diagnostics are analytical tools for identifying structural, tonal, and pacing problems in a draft. Where revision is about fixing problems, diagnostics is about finding them — systematically, before intuition alone would catch them.

Professional authors use diagnostics between drafting and revision to create a targeted revision plan rather than wading through the manuscript hoping to notice what's wrong. This phase provides structured methods for analyzing a draft across multiple dimensions.

## Diagnostic Dimensions

A manuscript can be analyzed along six axes. Each catches different categories of problems:

1. **Pacing & Tension** — Is the story moving at the right speed? Where does it stall or rush?
2. **Emotional Arc** — Does the reader's emotional experience build and release effectively?
3. **Character Consistency** — Do characters behave, speak, and think consistently with who they are?
4. **Information Flow** — Is the reader learning what they need to know, when they need to know it?
5. **Tonal Consistency** — Does the prose voice stay coherent, or does it drift?
6. **Structural Balance** — Are the parts of the novel proportioned well?

You don't have to run every diagnostic on every draft. Choose the ones that address your known weaknesses or the areas where something feels off but you can't articulate why.

## Diagnostic 1: Pacing & Tension Map

### Purpose
Identify chapters or sequences where momentum stalls, tension plateaus, or the pace rushes past moments that need room to breathe.

### Method

**Step 1: Chapter-by-chapter tension rating**

Go through each chapter and rate its tension level on a 1-5 scale:

| Rating | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Low tension — reflective, expository, or transitional |
| 2 | Mild tension — character development, relationship building, setup |
| 3 | Moderate tension — active conflict, stakes present, forward momentum |
| 4 | High tension — confrontation, revelation, significant turning point |
| 5 | Peak tension — climactic scene, crisis moment, maximum stakes |

**Step 2: Plot the curve**

List your chapters with their ratings. The shape should look roughly like this for a well-paced novel:

```
Ideal tension curve (approximate):

Act 1:  2 → 3 → 2 → 4 (inciting incident)
Act 2a: 3 → 2 → 3 → 4 (midpoint)
Act 2b: 3 → 4 → 3 → 5 → 2 (all-is-lost)
Act 3:  3 → 4 → 5 (climax) → 2 (resolution)
```

**Step 3: Identify problems**

- **Plateaus** — Three or more consecutive chapters at the same tension level. The story feels like it's treading water.
- **Missing valleys** — No low-tension chapters between high-tension ones. The reader gets exhausted without relief.
- **Premature peaks** — A tension-5 chapter too early in the manuscript. The climax has nowhere to go.
- **Late starts** — The first tension-4 moment doesn't arrive until 20%+ into the manuscript. The reader may not get there.
- **Rushed endings** — The climax and resolution happen in the same chapter or back-to-back. The payoff feels compressed.

### Output
A tension map with annotated problem areas and specific recommendations for each.

## Diagnostic 2: Emotional Arc Tracker

### Purpose
Ensure the reader's emotional journey is intentional and effective — that the novel creates the feelings you intend, in the right sequence.

### Method

**Step 1: Identify the intended emotional beats**

For each chapter or major scene, note:
- What emotion should the reader feel at the start?
- What emotion should they feel at the end?
- What's the dominant emotional register? (dread, tenderness, excitement, grief, humor, unease)

**Step 2: Check for emotional logic**

Emotions should flow naturally from one to the next. Abrupt shifts need to be earned:

- Grief → numbness → anger (natural progression)
- Grief → comedy → horror (jarring unless deliberately disorienting)
- Tension → relief → deeper tension (effective ratcheting)
- Tension → tension → tension → tension (exhausting)

**Step 3: Identify emotional dead zones**

Passages where no particular emotion is being generated — the prose is functional but flat. These often correspond to:
- Exposition dumps
- Travel or transition scenes written out of obligation
- Scenes where the author knows what happens but hasn't found the emotional core

**Step 4: Check the emotional climax**

- Does the emotional peak align with the plot climax? (It should, or there should be a deliberate reason it doesn't)
- Is the emotional payoff proportional to the buildup?
- Does the resolution provide emotional closure, or does it just stop?

### Output
An emotional arc map with dead zones flagged and misaligned beats identified.

## Diagnostic 3: Character Consistency Audit

### Purpose
Catch moments where characters act, speak, or think in ways that contradict their established patterns — unless the contradiction is intentional and motivated.

### Method

**Step 1: Create a character behavior baseline**

For each major character, list:
- Core traits (3-5 adjectives)
- Speech patterns (vocabulary, rhythm, verbal habits)
- Decision-making tendencies (impulsive/cautious, emotional/analytical, confrontational/avoidant)
- Knowledge boundaries (what they know and don't know at each point in the story)
- Emotional range (what emotions they express openly vs. suppress)

**Step 2: Scan for violations**

Read through the manuscript watching for moments where a character:
- Uses vocabulary or syntax inconsistent with their voice
- Makes a decision that contradicts their established tendencies without sufficient motivation
- Knows something they shouldn't know yet (information leak)
- Reacts emotionally in a way that doesn't match their personality
- Disappears from scenes where they should logically be present

**Step 3: Classify violations**

- **Unintentional inconsistency** — The author forgot or wasn't tracking. Needs fixing.
- **Unmotivated change** — The character acts differently but the manuscript doesn't explain why. Needs setup or foreshadowing.
- **Intentional break** — The character is deliberately acting against type, and the text signals this. No fix needed, but verify the signal is clear enough.

**Step 4: Check arc progression**

Characters should change, but gradually and causally:
- Is there a clear trigger for each shift in behavior?
- Does the change feel earned by what the character has experienced?
- Can you trace a logical line from who they are in chapter 1 to who they are in the final chapter?

### Output
A per-character consistency report with flagged violations and arc progression notes.

## Diagnostic 4: Information Flow Analysis

### Purpose
Ensure the reader receives information at the right time — not too early (kills suspense), not too late (creates confusion), and not redundantly (insults the reader).

### Method

**Step 1: Catalog key information**

List every piece of information the reader needs to understand the story:
- World-building facts (rules, history, geography)
- Character backstory (motivations, relationships, secrets)
- Plot mechanics (how things work, why events happen)
- Mystery/revelation elements (clues, red herrings, twists)

**Step 2: Map delivery points**

For each piece of information, note:
- When is it first introduced?
- How is it delivered? (dialogue, narration, action, discovery)
- Is it repeated? If so, where and why?
- When does the reader need it to understand what's happening?

**Step 3: Identify problems**

- **Front-loading** — Too much information delivered before the reader has a reason to care. Common in fantasy and sci-fi openings.
- **Information gaps** — The reader needs to know something to understand a scene, but it hasn't been established yet.
- **Redundant delivery** — The same information explained multiple times without new context. Trust the reader.
- **Buried revelations** — Important information delivered in a way that doesn't signal its importance. The reader skims past it.
- **Premature reveals** — Mysteries or secrets revealed before maximum tension has been built around them.
- **Exposition clusters** — Multiple pieces of information delivered in the same scene, creating a lecture feel.

**Step 4: Optimize timing**

For each problem, determine:
- Can this information be delivered later, closer to when it's needed?
- Can it be delivered through action or dialogue instead of narration?
- Can it be split across multiple scenes?
- Can it be cut entirely? (Does the reader actually need this?)

### Output
An information flow map with delivery timing issues flagged and redistribution recommendations.

## Diagnostic 5: Tonal Consistency Check

### Purpose
Detect passages where the prose voice drifts from the intended style — using Fiction Seeds as the reference standard.

### Method

**Step 1: Establish the reference**

Load the primary Fiction Seed for the manuscript (or the project manifest if using multiple Seeds). This is your tonal baseline.

**Step 2: Sample passages**

Select 5-10 passages from across the manuscript — at least one from each act, plus any sections that felt "off" during drafting. Choose passages of 300-500 words each.

**Step 3: Compare against the Seed**

For each passage, check:
- **Sentence length** — Does the average match the Seed's profile?
- **Vocabulary register** — Has the diction shifted up or down?
- **Figurative language density** — More or fewer metaphors/similes than intended?
- **Emotional distance** — Has the narrative pulled closer or farther from the character?
- **Rhythm** — Does it sound like the same narrator?

**Step 4: Identify drift patterns**

Common drift patterns:
- **Gradual formalization** — The prose gets stiffer and more "writerly" as the manuscript progresses. Often caused by fatigue or self-consciousness.
- **Tonal contamination** — The voice of whatever you were reading during drafting bleeds into your prose.
- **Scene-type drift** — Action scenes sound different from quiet scenes in ways that go beyond appropriate register shifts.
- **Revision scarring** — Heavily revised passages sound different from lightly revised ones because the editing voice overwrote the drafting voice.

**Step 5: Prescribe corrections**

For each drifted passage:
- Reload the appropriate Seed
- Note the specific dimensions that drifted
- Flag for targeted revision (don't rewrite now — just diagnose)

### Output
A tonal consistency report with drifted passages identified, drift type classified, and the relevant Seed referenced for correction.

## Diagnostic 6: Structural Balance Analysis

### Purpose
Check whether the novel's parts are proportioned effectively — that no act overstays its welcome, no subplot dominates inappropriately, and the story's weight is distributed intentionally.

### Method

**Step 1: Measure the parts**

Calculate word counts for:
- Each act (or major section)
- Each subplot thread
- Each POV character's total page time (if multi-POV)
- Each chapter

**Step 2: Check proportions**

**Act balance (rough guidelines, not rules):**
- Act 1: 20-25% of total word count
- Act 2: 50-55%
- Act 3: 20-25%

Significant deviations aren't automatically wrong, but they should be intentional.

**Subplot balance:**
- Does any subplot consume more page time than its importance warrants?
- Does any subplot disappear for long stretches? (If the reader forgets about it, it's been gone too long)
- Do subplots resolve before the main plot climax?

**POV balance (multi-POV only):**
- Does each POV character get enough page time to justify their perspective?
- Is the distribution intentional? (The protagonist might get 60%, others 20% each — that's fine if deliberate)
- Are there POV characters who could be cut or merged?

**Chapter length consistency:**
- Wild variation in chapter length can be effective or distracting
- Very long chapters in a short-chapter book (or vice versa) should be intentional
- Consistently long chapters may indicate scenes that need trimming

**Step 3: Identify imbalances**

- **Bloated Act 2** — The middle sags because it's carrying too much weight. Look for scenes to cut or compress.
- **Rushed Act 3** — The ending feels compressed. The climax and resolution may need more room.
- **Subplot dominance** — A secondary storyline has taken over. Either promote it to the main plot or trim it back.
- **POV imbalance** — A character's perspective isn't earning its page time. Consider cutting their POV or giving them more meaningful scenes.

### Output
A structural balance report with word count breakdowns, proportion analysis, and rebalancing recommendations.

## Running a Full Diagnostic

For a complete manuscript analysis, run the diagnostics in this order:

1. **Structural Balance** — Start with the big picture. If the proportions are wrong, other diagnostics will reflect that.
2. **Pacing & Tension** — Map the momentum. This reveals where the story drags or rushes.
3. **Emotional Arc** — Layer the emotional experience on top of the pacing map.
4. **Information Flow** — Check that the reader knows what they need to know, when they need to know it.
5. **Character Consistency** — Audit the cast for behavioral and voice coherence.
6. **Tonal Consistency** — Fine-grained prose analysis using Seeds as the reference.

A full diagnostic pass typically takes 1-2 days for a complete manuscript. The output is a revision plan — a prioritized list of problems to address, organized by severity and type.

## Diagnostic Report Template

After running diagnostics, compile findings into a single report:

```markdown
# Diagnostic Report: {Project Title}

Date: {date}
Draft: {draft number}
Word count: {total}

## Critical Issues (fix first)
- {Issue}: {Location} — {Brief description and recommendation}

## Significant Issues (fix in revision)
- {Issue}: {Location} — {Brief description and recommendation}

## Minor Issues (fix in polish)
- {Issue}: {Location} — {Brief description and recommendation}

## Strengths (preserve in revision)
- {What's working well and should not be disrupted}

## Revision Priority Order
1. {First thing to address}
2. {Second thing to address}
3. ...
```

## Connection to Other Phases

- **Plot Structure** — Structural balance and pacing diagnostics validate your outline's execution
- **Character Creation** — Character consistency audit checks profiles against the draft
- **Style & Tone** — Tonal consistency check uses your style decisions as the baseline
- **Fiction Seeds / Seed Library** — Seeds serve as the reference standard for tonal diagnostics
- **Revision & Editing** — Diagnostic output becomes the revision plan; run diagnostics before revising, not during

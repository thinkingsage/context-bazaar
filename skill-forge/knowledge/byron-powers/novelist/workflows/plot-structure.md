# Plot Structure & Outlining

## Overview

Structure is the skeleton of your novel. It determines how information is revealed, how tension builds, and how the story delivers its emotional payload. This phase helps you shape your narrative arc and create a working outline — whether you prefer detailed blueprints or loose roadmaps.

## Workflow: Shaping Your Story

### Step 1: Choose a Structural Framework

No single framework is "correct." Pick one that fits your story's needs:

**Three-Act Structure (most common):**
- Act 1 (25%) — Setup: Introduce world, characters, and the inciting incident
- Act 2 (50%) — Confrontation: Rising stakes, complications, midpoint shift, all-is-lost moment
- Act 3 (25%) — Resolution: Climax, falling action, denouement

**The Hero's Journey (mythic stories):**
- Ordinary World → Call to Adventure → Refusal → Meeting the Mentor → Crossing the Threshold → Tests/Allies/Enemies → Approach → Ordeal → Reward → The Road Back → Resurrection → Return with Elixir

**Seven-Point Structure (tight plotting):**
- Hook → Plot Turn 1 → Pinch Point 1 → Midpoint → Pinch Point 2 → Plot Turn 2 → Resolution

**Save the Cat (beat-driven):**
- 15 beats from Opening Image to Final Image, each with a specific purpose and approximate page count

**Kishotenketsu (non-confrontational):**
- Introduction → Development → Twist → Reconciliation
- Useful for literary fiction where conflict isn't the primary engine

**No framework (discovery writers):**
- Write from character and situation, find structure in revision
- Higher risk, but can produce organic, surprising narratives

### Step 2: Identify Key Turning Points

Regardless of framework, every novel needs these structural moments:

**The Inciting Incident:**
- What event disrupts the protagonist's normal life?
- When does it happen? (Ideally within the first 10-15% of the manuscript)
- Why can't the protagonist ignore it?

**The First Plot Point (End of Act 1):**
- What commits the protagonist to the central conflict?
- What choice do they make that there's no coming back from?

**The Midpoint:**
- What shifts the story's direction or raises the stakes dramatically?
- Does the protagonist shift from reactive to proactive (or vice versa)?
- What new information changes everything?

**The Crisis / All-Is-Lost Moment:**
- What is the protagonist's lowest point?
- What seems impossible to overcome?
- What internal change must happen for them to move forward?

**The Climax:**
- What is the final confrontation or decisive moment?
- How does the protagonist's arc culminate?
- How does the theme resolve (or deliberately remain unresolved)?

**The Resolution:**
- What does the new normal look like?
- What has changed — in the world and in the protagonist?
- What emotional note does the story end on?

### Step 3: Build Subplots

Subplots add depth and prevent the main plot from feeling one-dimensional:

- **Character subplots** — A supporting character's arc that mirrors or contrasts the protagonist's
- **Relationship subplots** — Romance, friendship, rivalry that complicates the main conflict
- **Thematic subplots** — A secondary storyline that explores the theme from a different angle
- **Mystery/revelation subplots** — Information parceled out over time that recontextualizes the main plot

**Rules for subplots:**
- Each subplot should connect to the main plot or theme
- Subplots should have their own mini-arcs (beginning, middle, end)
- Resolve subplots before or during the climax, not after
- If a subplot doesn't serve the story, cut it

### Step 4: Create Your Outline

Choose your outlining depth:

**Detailed Outline (plotters):**
- Chapter-by-chapter breakdown
- Scene list with purpose, POV, and key beats for each
- Specific dialogue or moments you want to hit
- Timeline with dates if relevant

**Loose Outline (plantsers):**
- Key turning points identified
- General direction for each act
- Character arcs mapped at high level
- Flexibility for discovery during drafting

**Signpost Outline (pantsers who want guardrails):**
- Only the major turning points defined
- Everything between them is discovered in the writing
- Enough structure to prevent wandering, enough freedom to surprise yourself

### Step 5: Validate the Structure

Before moving to drafting, stress-test your outline:

- **Causality check:** Does each event cause the next, or are things just happening in sequence?
- **Escalation check:** Do stakes increase steadily, or do they plateau?
- **Character agency check:** Is the protagonist driving the plot through choices, or being dragged through events?
- **Pacing check:** Are there long stretches without tension or turning points?
- **Theme check:** Does the structure support the thematic argument?

## Techniques

### The Index Card Method
Write each scene on an index card (or digital equivalent). Rearrange them to test different orderings. This makes structure tangible and movable.

### The Reverse Outline
Start with the ending and work backward. Knowing where you're going makes it easier to build the path there.

### The "Therefore/But" Test
Connect each plot point with "therefore" (consequence) or "but" (complication). If you can only connect them with "and then," the plot lacks causality.

### The Subplot Weave
Map your subplots alongside the main plot on a timeline. Check that they intersect at key moments and don't disappear for too long.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A chosen structural framework (or conscious decision to work without one)
- Key turning points identified and placed
- Subplots defined with connections to the main plot
- A working outline at your preferred level of detail
- Confidence in the story's shape and pacing

## Next Phase

With your structure in place, move to **scene-drafting** to start writing the manuscript.

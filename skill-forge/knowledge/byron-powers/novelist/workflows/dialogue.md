# Dialogue

## Overview

Dialogue is where characters come alive. It's the most direct way readers experience your cast — their intelligence, their humor, their evasions, their pain. Great dialogue does multiple jobs at once: it reveals character, advances plot, creates tension, and controls pacing. This phase dives deep into the craft of writing conversations that feel authentic and do real work in your story.

## Workflow: Crafting Dialogue

### Step 1: Understand What Dialogue Is For

Every line of dialogue should serve at least one of these purposes:

- **Reveal character** — How someone speaks tells us who they are
- **Advance the plot** — New information, decisions, confrontations
- **Create conflict** — Characters wanting different things in the same conversation
- **Establish relationships** — Power dynamics, intimacy, distance, history
- **Build subtext** — The gap between what's said and what's meant
- **Control pacing** — Dialogue reads faster than description; use it to accelerate

If a line of dialogue doesn't serve any of these, cut it.

### Step 2: Give Each Character a Distinct Voice

Readers should be able to identify who's speaking without dialogue tags. Build distinctiveness through:

**Vocabulary:**
- Education level — A professor and a mechanic choose different words
- Regional or cultural background — Idioms, slang, expressions
- Profession — Jargon, technical language, occupational habits
- Generation — A teenager and a retiree don't sound the same

**Syntax and rhythm:**
- Sentence length — Some people speak in fragments; others in elaborate constructions
- Directness — Does this character get to the point or circle around it?
- Formality — Contractions or full forms? Slang or proper grammar?

**Conversational habits:**
- Does this character ask questions or make statements?
- Do they interrupt or wait their turn?
- Do they deflect with humor, silence, or aggression?
- What topics do they avoid? What do they always bring up?

**Exercise:** Write the same piece of news delivered by three different characters. If all three sound the same, their voices aren't distinct enough.

### Step 3: Master Subtext

The most powerful dialogue happens beneath the surface. Subtext is the gap between what characters say and what they actually mean or feel.

**Techniques for creating subtext:**

- **Avoidance** — Characters talk about anything except the real issue. A couple arguing about dishes is really arguing about respect.
- **Contradiction** — What a character says contradicts what they do. "I'm fine," she said, slamming the cabinet shut.
- **Deflection** — Answering a question with a different question, changing the subject, making a joke to dodge vulnerability.
- **Loaded language** — Words that carry extra weight because of shared history. "Just like your mother" is never just a comparison.
- **Silence** — What a character doesn't say can be louder than what they do. A pause, a non-answer, a subject change.

**The iceberg principle:** The reader should sense that there's more beneath the conversation than what's on the surface. You don't need to explain the subtext — trust the reader to feel it.

### Step 4: Write Natural-Sounding Dialogue

Real speech and fictional dialogue aren't the same thing. Real speech is full of filler, repetition, and dead ends. Fictional dialogue should feel natural while being more purposeful and compressed.

**Making dialogue feel real:**
- Use contractions (unless the character wouldn't)
- Allow interruptions and incomplete thoughts
- Let characters mishear or misunderstand each other
- Include the occasional non-sequitur or tangent (sparingly)
- People don't always answer the question they were asked

**Avoiding common traps:**
- **"As you know, Bob"** — Characters telling each other things they both already know for the reader's benefit. Find another way to convey the information.
- **On-the-nose dialogue** — Characters stating exactly what they feel. Real people rarely do this. "I'm angry at you because you betrayed my trust" → Show the anger through how they speak, not what they declare.
- **Identical voices** — If you can swap dialogue between characters and nothing feels wrong, the voices aren't distinct enough.
- **Exposition dumps** — Long speeches explaining backstory or world-building. Break information into small pieces across multiple conversations.

### Step 5: Handle Dialogue Mechanics

The technical side of writing dialogue matters for readability:

**Dialogue tags:**
- "Said" is invisible — use it as your default
- "Asked" is fine for questions
- Avoid creative tags: "exclaimed," "opined," "queried," "ejaculated" — they draw attention to themselves
- Action beats can replace tags entirely: She set down her coffee. "That's not what happened."

**Action beats:**
- Ground dialogue in physical reality — characters should be doing things while they talk
- Use beats to control pacing — a beat slows the conversation, creating a pause
- Avoid meaningless beats (nodding, smiling, shrugging) — make physical actions reveal character or emotion

**Formatting:**
- New speaker, new paragraph — always
- Keep speeches short — long monologues lose the reader
- Use paragraph breaks within long speeches to give the reader breathing room

**Dialect and accent:**
- Suggest dialect through word choice and syntax, not phonetic spelling
- "I ain't going" works; "Ah ain't goin' nowheres" is hard to read
- A light touch is more effective than heavy transcription
- Consistency matters — once you establish a character's speech pattern, maintain it

### Step 6: Write Dialogue-Heavy Scenes

Some scenes are primarily conversation. These need special attention:

**Conflict-driven conversations:**
- Each character enters with a goal
- Goals should be in opposition (or at least in tension)
- The conversation should escalate — each exchange raises the stakes
- Someone should "win" or the dynamic should shift by the end

**Information-exchange conversations:**
- The reader needs to learn something, but the conversation shouldn't feel like a lecture
- Give the information-holder a reason to be reluctant or selective
- Let the listener react, question, and challenge — don't make them a passive audience
- Break up information with emotional responses and tangential moments

**Intimate conversations:**
- Vulnerability is hard — characters should resist it before giving in
- Silence and pauses carry enormous weight
- Physical proximity and touch can say what words can't
- The most intimate moments are often the quietest

## Techniques

### The Eavesdropping Exercise
Go to a public place and listen to real conversations (ethically — don't record). Notice how people actually talk: the interruptions, the tangents, the way they circle topics. Then write a fictional scene inspired by the rhythms you heard.

### The Conflict Ladder
Before writing a confrontation scene, list the escalation points. Start with polite disagreement and end with the worst thing either character could say. You don't have to use every rung, but knowing the full ladder helps you control the escalation.

### The Silent Scene
Write a scene where two characters need to communicate something important but can't or won't speak. Use action, gesture, and environment to convey what dialogue would normally carry. This strengthens your ability to write subtext when you return to dialogue.

### The Transcript Method
Write a scene as pure dialogue first — no tags, no beats, no description. Just the words spoken. This forces you to make the dialogue carry everything. Then layer in the other elements only where needed.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Talking heads** — Dialogue floating in a void with no physical grounding. Anchor conversations in setting and action.
- **Symmetrical exchanges** — Characters taking neat, equal turns. Real conversations are messy — people interrupt, talk over each other, and circle back.
- **Placeholder dialogue** — Lines that move the plot but don't sound like anyone real would say them. Every line should sound like it comes from a specific person.
- **Over-attribution** — Too many dialogue tags and beats. Once the rhythm of a two-person conversation is established, you can drop tags for several exchanges.
- **Monologuing** — Characters giving speeches. Break long statements into exchanges. Let other characters react, interrupt, or redirect.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- Distinct voice profiles for each major character's speech patterns
- Subtext strategies for key relationships and conversations
- Dialogue samples that demonstrate each character's voice
- Confidence in writing conversations that do real narrative work

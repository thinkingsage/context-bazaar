# Career Strategy

## Overview

A single book is a project. A career is a portfolio of projects, relationships, and decisions that compound over time. This phase covers the long game — multi-book planning, income diversification, rights management, and the strategic thinking that turns a published author into a sustainable creative career.

### Step 2: Multi-Book Planning

**The career arc:**
Most successful author careers follow a pattern:
1. **Debut** — Establish yourself. Build an audience. Learn the industry.
2. **Growth** — Expand your readership. Refine your brand. Negotiate better deals.
3. **Establishment** — Consistent output. Loyal readership. Industry relationships.
4. **Legacy** — Backlist sales. Mentoring. Selective projects.

**Book sequencing strategy:**

| Strategy | Description | Best For |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| Series | Connected books with recurring characters/world | Genre fiction, fantasy, mystery |
| Standalone sequence | Unconnected books in the same genre | Literary fiction, thriller |
| Genre pivot | Deliberate shift to a new genre | Authors seeking creative renewal |
| Parallel tracks | Multiple genres simultaneously (possibly with pen names) | Prolific authors with diverse interests |

**Planning your next book:**
- Start thinking about your next book while the current one is in production
- Discuss direction with your agent before committing significant time
- Consider market timing — what will the landscape look like when this book publishes (12-18 months from now)?
- Balance what you want to write with what's strategically smart — but never write something you don't care about just for market reasons

### Step 3: Income Diversification

**Writing income is unpredictable.** Advances vary, royalties are delayed, and most books don't earn out. A sustainable career requires multiple income streams.

**Income sources for authors:**

| Source | Effort | Income Potential | Reliability |
|--------|--------|-----------------|-------------|
| Advances | Low (per book) | Variable ($5K-$500K+) | Per deal |
| Royalties | None (passive) | Low-moderate | Unpredictable |
| Foreign rights sales | Low (agent handles) | Moderate | Per deal |
| Film/TV options | Low (agent handles) | Variable ($5K-$100K+) | Rare |
| Audio rights | Low (agent handles) | Moderate | Growing market |
| Speaking fees | Medium | $500-$10,000+ per event | Seasonal |
| Teaching/workshops | Medium-high | $1,000-$10,000+ per engagement | Recurring |
| Freelance writing | Medium | Variable | Reliable if cultivated |
| Consulting | Medium | Variable | Niche-dependent |
| Patreon/Substack | High (ongoing content) | $100-$10,000+/month | Requires audience |
| Self-published backlist | Medium (initial setup) | Variable | Passive once established |

### Step 4: Rights Management

Your intellectual property is your most valuable long-term asset. Manage it actively.

**Rights inventory:**

```markdown
# Rights Inventory: {Author Name}

## {Book Title 1}
- Publisher: {name}
- Contract date: {date}
- Rights granted: {list — print, ebook, audio, territory}
- Rights retained: {list}
- Reversion clause: {summary of terms}
- Option clause: {summary}
- Foreign rights sold: {list of territories and publishers}
- Film/TV status: {optioned / available / sold}
- Audio status: {produced / available / rights with publisher}

## {Book Title 2}
{Same structure}
```

**Rights management actions:**
- Review your rights inventory annually
- Request reversion of rights when sales fall below contract thresholds
- Ensure your agent is actively marketing unsold subsidiary rights
- Track option clause deadlines
- Consider self-publishing reverted titles

### Step 5: Relationship Management

Publishing is a relationship business. Nurture your professional network:

**Key relationships to maintain:**
- Your agent (regular communication, career discussions)
- Your editor(s) (professional, responsive, collaborative)
- Your publicist (at the publisher and/or independent)
- Fellow authors (mutual support, cross-promotion, community)
- Booksellers (especially independent bookstore staff)
- Librarians (they're powerful advocates)
- Media contacts (journalists, podcasters, reviewers)
- Event organizers (festival directors, conference planners)

**Relationship maintenance:**
- Respond promptly to professional communications
- Show up for other authors' launches and events
- Recommend books you genuinely love
- Be easy to work with — publishing is small, and reputation travels
- Express gratitude regularly and specifically
- Don't burn bridges, even when frustrated

### Step 6: Navigating Career Challenges

**When a book underperforms:**
- Don't panic. Most careers include books that underperform.
- Analyze why (market timing, positioning, execution, competition) without spiraling
- Discuss with your agent — they've seen this before
- Focus on the next book. The best response to a disappointing book is a great next one.

**When you want to change direction:**
- Discuss with your agent before committing
- Consider whether a pen name is needed or whether your audience will follow
- Plan the transition over 2-3 books rather than making an abrupt shift
- Be honest with your publisher about your creative direction

**When the agent relationship isn't working:**
- Have an honest conversation first — many issues are fixable
- If it's not fixable, part ways professionally (follow the termination clause in your agreement)
- Don't badmouth your former agent — the industry is small
- Take time to find the right next agent rather than rushing

**When you're burned out:**
- Take a break. A sustainable career requires rest.
- Reduce non-writing obligations (social media, events, freelance)
- Reconnect with why you started writing
- Consider whether your pace is sustainable long-term
- Talk to other authors — burnout is common and not a personal failure

## Techniques

### The Backlist Compound Method
Every book you publish adds to your backlist. Backlist sales compound over time — a reader who discovers your latest book may buy everything you've written. Invest in keeping your backlist available, discoverable, and well-presented.

### The Two-Track Method
Always have two tracks running: the current book (writing/production) and the career (strategy/relationships/platform). Neglecting either one creates problems. Schedule dedicated time for both.

### The Peer Cohort Method
Find 3-5 authors at a similar career stage. Meet regularly (monthly or quarterly) to share experiences, challenges, and strategies. A peer group provides perspective, accountability, and emotional support that no agent or editor can.

### The Optionality Method
Make career decisions that preserve options rather than closing them. Retain rights when possible. Build relationships across the industry. Develop skills (speaking, teaching, editing) that create alternative income paths. The more options you have, the more resilient your career.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:

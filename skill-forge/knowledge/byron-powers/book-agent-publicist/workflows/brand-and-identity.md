# Brand & Identity

## Overview

Your author brand is the promise readers associate with your name. It's not a logo or a color palette — it's the consistent experience people have when they encounter you, your books, and your public presence. A clear brand makes you memorable, helps readers find you, and gives your marketing efforts coherence. This phase defines your brand and creates the assets to express it consistently.

## Workflow: Defining Your Author Brand

### Step 1: Identify Your Brand Core

Your brand starts with three questions:

**What do you write?**
Not just genre — the specific experience your books deliver. "Atmospheric literary thrillers set in small towns" is a brand. "Thrillers" is a category.

**Who do you write for?**
Your ideal reader, described specifically. "Women 25-45 who love Tana French and want smart, slow-burn suspense" is a reader profile. "People who like books" is not.

**Why should they care?**
What makes your work distinctive. Your unique perspective, voice, expertise, or approach. This is the hardest question and the most important one.

**Brand core template:**

```markdown
# Author Brand Core: {Author Name}

## Brand Promise
{One sentence: what readers can expect every time they pick up your book}

## Genre Position
{Your specific niche within your genre}

## Ideal Reader
{Specific description of who your books are for}

## Differentiator
{What makes your work distinctive — your unique angle, voice, or expertise}

## Brand Personality
{3-5 adjectives that describe the feeling of your brand}
Examples: warm, sharp, atmospheric, witty, unflinching, cerebral, accessible, dark, hopeful

## Tagline (optional)
{A short phrase that captures your brand — for website headers, social media bios, etc.}
```

### Step 2: Define Your Visual Identity

Visual consistency makes you recognizable across platforms.

**Author photo:**
- Professional quality (hire a photographer)
- Consistent with your brand personality (literary = thoughtful pose; thriller = intense; humor = approachable smile)
- Use the same photo across all platforms for recognition
- Update every 2-3 years or when your look changes significantly

**Color palette:**
- Choose 2-3 colors that reflect your brand personality
- Use them consistently on your website, social media graphics, and marketing materials
- Don't overthink this — consistency matters more than perfection

**Typography:**
- Choose 1-2 fonts for your website and marketing materials
- One for headings, one for body text
- Keep it readable and consistent with your genre's visual conventions

**Visual identity document:**

```markdown

- Accent: {color and hex code}

- {3-5 reference images or descriptions that capture the visual feeling of your brand}
```

### Step 3: Craft Your Messaging

**The elevator pitch (10 seconds):**
"I write [genre/type] about [core theme] for readers who love [comp reference]."

Example: "I write atmospheric mysteries set in the Pacific Northwest for readers who love Tana French and want their suspense slow and smart."

**The bio pitch (30 seconds):**
Your elevator pitch expanded with one personal detail and one credential.

Example: "I'm a former park ranger turned mystery writer. I write atmospheric crime fiction set in the Pacific Northwest, where the landscape is as much a character as the detective. My debut, [TITLE], was a [credential]. I write for readers who want their suspense slow, smart, and soaked in rain."

**The full bio (1-2 paragraphs):**
Your bio pitch expanded with publication history, relevant background, and a personal touch.

**Messaging consistency rules:**
- Use the same core language across all platforms (website, social media bios, press materials)
- Adapt length and tone for each platform, but keep the core message identical
- Update messaging when you publish a new book or hit a significant milestone
- Your genre description should be consistent everywhere — don't call yourself a "thriller writer" on Twitter and a "literary suspense author" on your website

## Pen Names and Multiple Brands

If you write in multiple genres, you may need separate brands:

**When to use a pen name:**
- Your genres have very different audiences (e.g., children's books and horror)
- Reader expectations would be confused by genre-hopping under one name
- You want to keep professional and creative writing separate

**When one name works:**
- Your genres are adjacent (e.g., thriller and mystery)
- Your voice is consistent across genres
- Your audience is likely to follow you across genres

**Managing multiple brands:**
- Each pen name needs its own website, social media, and newsletter
- This is significant work — don't create a pen name unless the genre gap truly requires it
- Some authors use their real name for one genre and a pen name for another
- Be prepared for the secret to eventually come out — plan for that

## Techniques

### The Reader Perspective Method
Describe your brand from a reader's point of view: "When I pick up a [Your Name] book, I know I'm going to get..." If you can't finish that sentence clearly, your brand isn't defined yet.

### The Bookshelf Test
Imagine your books on a shelf together. Do they look like they belong to the same author? Do the covers, titles, and descriptions create a coherent impression? If not, your brand may need tightening.

### The Subtraction Method
List everything you could include in your brand (topics, tones, visual styles, audiences). Then remove everything that isn't essential. A strong brand is defined as much by what it excludes as what it includes.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A brand core document (promise, position, reader, differentiator, personality)
- A visual identity guide (colors, fonts, photo, mood)
- Messaging at three lengths (elevator, bio, full)
- A consistency audit of all online platforms
- A brand guidelines document for collaborators
- A decision on pen names (if applicable)

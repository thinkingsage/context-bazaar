# Deal Negotiation

## Overview

A book deal is a business transaction with long-term financial and creative implications. Your agent handles the negotiation, but you need to understand the mechanics — advances, royalties, deal structures, and the financial realities of traditional publishing — to make informed decisions and be an effective partner in the process.

## Workflow: From Offer to Signed Deal

### Step 1: Understand the Offer

When an editor makes an offer, your agent will present it to you. Here's what the key terms mean:

**The advance:**
The advance is an upfront payment against future royalties. You don't earn additional royalties until the book "earns out" — meaning royalty earnings exceed the advance.

| Advance Level | Approximate Range | What It Signals |
|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| "Nice deal" | $1 - $49,000 | Standard for debut authors |
| "Very nice deal" | $50,000 - $99,000 | Strong interest, competitive |
| "Good deal" | $100,000 - $250,000 | High expectations, likely auction |
| "Significant deal" | $251,000 - $499,000 | Major acquisition |
| "Major deal" | $500,000+ | Blockbuster expectations |

(These are Publishers Marketplace categories. Actual amounts vary by genre, author platform, and market conditions.)

**Advance payment schedule:**
Advances are typically paid in installments:
- **Two-part:** Half on signing, half on delivery/acceptance of manuscript
- **Three-part:** Third on signing, third on delivery, third on publication
- **Four-part:** Quarter on signing, quarter on delivery, quarter on publication, quarter on paperback publication

More installments means slower payment. Negotiate for fewer installments when possible.

**Royalty rates (standard for hardcover/trade):**

| Format | Standard Rate | Escalation |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| Hardcover | 10% of list price | 12.5% after 5,000 copies, 15% after 10,000 |
| Trade paperback | 7.5% of list price | Sometimes escalates to 10% |
| Mass market paperback | 8% of list price | Sometimes escalates to 10% |
| E-book | 25% of net receipts | Rarely escalates |
| Audiobook | 25% of net receipts | Varies |

**"List price" vs. "net receipts":**
- List price = the cover price. Your royalty is calculated on this regardless of discounts.
- Net receipts = what the publisher actually receives after retailer discounts (typically 40-55% less than list price). 25% of net is significantly less than 25% of list.

### Step 2: Evaluate the Offer

Don't evaluate an offer on advance alone. Consider the full picture:

**Financial factors:**
- Advance amount and payment schedule
- Royalty rates and escalation thresholds
- E-book and audio royalty rates (these are increasingly important)
- Sub-rights splits (what percentage you keep vs. the publisher)

**Strategic factors:**
- Imprint and editor reputation
- Marketing and publicity commitment (ask specifically what they plan)
- Publication timeline
- Print run expectations
- Series potential and option terms

**Creative factors:**
- Editorial vision — does the editor understand your book?
- Cover design input — how much say will you have?
- Title approval — can the publisher change your title without consent?

### Step 3: The Auction

If multiple editors want your book, your agent may run an auction:

**Auction types:**

| Type | How It Works |
|------|-------------|
| Best bids | All editors submit their best offer by a deadline. Highest (or best overall) wins. |
| Round-robin | Editors bid in rounds, with the option to raise or drop out each round. |
| Pre-empt | One editor makes a high offer to take the book off the table before the auction. |

**Pre-empt considerations:**
- A pre-empt is flattering and eliminates uncertainty
- But it may leave money on the table if other editors would have bid higher
- Your agent will advise on whether to accept or proceed to auction
- There's no obligation to accept a pre-empt

**During an auction:**
- Your agent manages all communication with editors
- You may be asked for your preference if offers are close in value but differ in other terms
- The highest advance isn't always the best deal — consider the full package
- Auctions typically resolve within 1-3 days

### Step 4: Multi-Book Deals

Publishers sometimes offer multi-book deals, especially for series or authors they want to invest in:

**Types:**
- **Two-book deal:** The current book plus the next one (which may be unwritten)
- **Three-book deal:** Common for planned trilogies or series
- **Option deal:** Publisher has the right to make an offer on your next book before you shop it elsewhere

**Pros of multi-book deals:**
- Financial security (larger total advance)
- Publisher commitment to building your career
- Guaranteed publication for the next book(s)

**Cons of multi-book deals:**
- Lower per-book advance than you might get negotiating individually
- Locked into a publisher even if the relationship sours
- Creative pressure to deliver books that fit the deal's expectations
- If Book 1 underperforms, Books 2-3 may get less marketing support

### Step 5: What Your Agent Negotiates

Beyond the advance, your agent negotiates terms that affect your career for years:

**Key negotiation points:**
- Advance amount and payment schedule
- Royalty rates and escalation
- Rights granted (territory, format, language)
- Sub-rights splits
- Option clause scope and terms
- Non-compete clause limitations
- Reversion clause triggers
- Marketing and publicity commitments
- Cover and title consultation
- Delivery and acceptance terms
- Out-of-print definition and reversion

Your agent handles these negotiations. Your job is to communicate your priorities clearly: "I care most about retaining audio rights" or "Marketing commitment matters more to me than a higher advance."

## Financial Realities

**Most books don't earn out their advance.** This is normal. The advance is your guaranteed payment. Earning out means the book exceeded the publisher's financial projections — a good sign, but not earning out doesn't mean the book failed.

**Royalty statements** arrive twice a year (typically). They're notoriously confusing. Your agent should review them and explain any questions.

**Reserve against returns:** Publishers hold back a percentage of royalties (typically 25-30%) as a reserve against bookstore returns. This money is released over 2-4 royalty periods.

**The math of publishing:**
- A $50,000 advance on a $27 hardcover at 10% royalty = $2.70 per copy
- Earn-out requires selling ~18,500 copies
- Average literary fiction hardcover sells 3,000-5,000 copies
- This is why most books don't earn out — and why the advance matters

## Techniques

### The Priority Stack Method
Before your agent negotiates, rank your priorities: advance size, royalty rates, rights retention, marketing commitment, creative control, publication timeline. Your agent can't optimize for everything — knowing your priorities helps them negotiate what matters most to you.

### The Comparable Deal Method
Ask your agent about recent deals for comparable books. This gives you realistic expectations and negotiating benchmarks.

### The Long-Term Math Method
Calculate the total potential value of a deal over 5-10 years, not just the advance. A lower advance with better royalty rates and rights retention may be worth more long-term than a higher advance with worse terms.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A clear understanding of your offer(s) and what each term means
- A priority list communicated to your agent
- An informed decision on which offer to accept (if multiple)
- Realistic financial expectations for the deal
- Readiness to move into contract review

# Media & Press

## Overview

Media coverage doesn't come from having a good book — it comes from having a good pitch. Journalists, podcasters, and reviewers are drowning in pitches. Yours needs to be specific, timely, and tailored to their audience. This phase covers building a press kit, pitching effectively, preparing for interviews, and building media relationships that last beyond a single book.

## Workflow: Getting Press Coverage

### Step 1: Build Your Press Kit

A press kit is a one-stop resource for anyone covering your book. Have it ready before you pitch anyone.

**Press kit contents:**

| Item | Format | Purpose |
|------|--------|---------|
| Press release | 1 page, PDF and plain text | Formal announcement of the book |
| Author bio | Short (50 words) and long (250 words) | For different editorial needs |
| Author photos | High-res JPG, multiple options | For print and online use |
| Book cover image | High-res JPG and PNG | For articles and social media |
| Book fact sheet | 1 page | Title, pub date, ISBN, price, publisher, page count |
| Sample interview Q&A | 10-15 questions with answers | Makes the journalist's job easier |
| Excerpt | 1-2 pages of compelling prose | Gives a taste of the writing |
| Blurbs/endorsements | Collected quotes | Social proof |
| Contact information | Email, phone, social media | How to reach you |

**Where to host it:**
- A dedicated page on your author website (e.g., yourname.com/press)
- A shared folder (Google Drive, Dropbox) with a clean link
- Both — website for discovery, shared folder for high-res downloads

### Step 2: Build Your Media List

**Categories of media targets:**

**Tier 1 — High impact, hard to get:**
- National newspapers and magazines (book review sections)
- Major literary podcasts
- National TV and radio book segments
- Major online publications with book coverage

**Tier 2 — Moderate impact, achievable:**
- Regional newspapers and magazines
- Genre-specific podcasts and blogs
- Online book review outlets
- Industry publications

**Tier 3 — Niche but valuable:**
- Local media (newspapers, radio, TV)
- Subject-matter media (if your book touches a specific topic)
- Book influencers (BookTok, Bookstagram, BookTube)
- Newsletter features and author spotlights
- Book club recommendation lists

**For each media target, document:**

```markdown
# Media Contact: {Outlet Name}

Contact: {journalist/host name}
Email: {email}
Type: {print / online / podcast / broadcast / influencer}
Tier: {1 / 2 / 3}
Audience: {who reads/listens}
Relevant coverage: {recent pieces similar to what you'd pitch}
Pitch angle: {which of your angles fits this outlet}
Lead time: {how far in advance they need pitches}
Status: {not pitched / pitched / confirmed / declined}
```

### Step 3: Craft Your Pitches

**The pitch email structure:**

```
Subject: {Specific, compelling subject line — not "Book Pitch"}

Dear {Name},

{Paragraph 1: The hook — why this story matters to their audience, right now. 2-3 sentences.}

{Paragraph 2: The book — what it is, one sentence. Why it's relevant to the hook. 2-3 sentences.}

{Paragraph 3: The author — why you're credible and interesting. 1-2 sentences.}

{Paragraph 4: The ask — what you're proposing (review, interview, excerpt, feature). Attach or link the press kit. 1-2 sentences.}

Best regards,
{Your name}
{Your website}
```

**Pitch rules:**
- **Personalize every pitch.** Reference the journalist's recent work. Show you read their outlet.
- **Lead with the story, not the book.** Journalists cover stories, not products.
- **Keep it under 200 words.** Journalists scan, they don't read.
- **One pitch per email.** Don't pitch multiple angles in one message.
- **Include the press kit link.** Don't make them ask for materials.
- **Follow up once.** One follow-up after 7-10 days. After that, move on.

**Pitch angle examples:**

Instead of: "I wrote a novel about climate change."

Try:
- "A novel that imagines Miami in 2045 — and the real science says it's not far off" (timely hook)
- "Why a marine biologist turned to fiction to tell the story science papers can't" (personal angle)
- "The three novels reshaping how we think about climate fiction" (trend piece — position yourself alongside others)

### Step 4: Prepare for Interviews

**Before any interview:**
- Research the interviewer and their style
- Listen to or read their recent interviews
- Prepare 3-5 key talking points you want to hit regardless of questions
- Practice transitioning from any question to your talking points
- Have specific anecdotes ready (stories are more memorable than abstractions)

**Interview talking points template:**

```markdown
# Interview Prep: {Outlet/Interviewer}

## Key Messages (hit at least 3 of these)
1. {Core message about the book}
2. {Personal story that connects to the book}
3. {Timely or surprising angle}
4. {What readers will take away}
5. {What you're working on next — if appropriate}

## Anticipated Questions
- {Likely question} → {Your answer, including bridge to a key message}
- {Likely question} → {Your answer}
- {Difficult question you hope they don't ask} → {Your prepared answer anyway}

## Anecdotes Ready
- {Story 1: brief, vivid, relevant}
- {Story 2: brief, vivid, relevant}

## Logistics
- Date/time: {when}
- Format: {phone / video / in-person / written}
- Duration: {expected length}
- Will they send questions in advance? {yes/no}
```

**During the interview:**
- Be conversational, not rehearsed
- Answer the question asked, then bridge to your talking point
- Use specific details and stories, not generalities
- Mention the book title naturally (2-3 times is enough)
- If you don't know something, say so — don't bluff

**After the interview:**
- Send a thank-you email within 24 hours
- Share the published piece on your social media and tag the journalist
- Add the journalist to your ongoing media contacts for future books

### Step 5: Manage Reviews

**Soliciting reviews:**
- Send ARCs to trade review outlets 4 months before publication (Kirkus, Publishers Weekly, Booklist, Library Journal)
- Send ARCs to consumer reviewers and influencers 2-3 months before publication
- Include a brief, personalized note with each ARC
- Follow up once if you haven't heard back

**Handling reviews:**
- Positive reviews: Share widely, quote in marketing materials (with permission if needed), thank the reviewer
- Mixed reviews: Share the positive parts, learn from the criticism, don't engage publicly with the negative
- Negative reviews: Do not respond. Ever. Not even to correct factual errors (have your publicist or publisher handle that). Responding to negative reviews never ends well.

**Review outlets by type:**

| Outlet Type | Lead Time | How to Submit |
|-------------|-----------|---------------|
| Trade reviews (Kirkus, PW) | 4+ months | Publisher submits; you can also submit independently |
| Newspaper book sections | 2-3 months | Pitch the books editor |
| Online book outlets | 1-2 months | Pitch the editor or reviewer |
| Podcasts | 1-2 months | Pitch the host |
| Book influencers | 1-2 months | DM or email with ARC offer |
| Amazon/Goodreads readers | Post-publication | Encourage readers to review |

## Techniques

### The Newsjacking Method
Monitor current events for connections to your book's themes. When a relevant news story breaks, pitch media with your book as expert commentary or a fictional exploration of the issue. Timeliness dramatically increases pitch success rates.

### The Podcast Ladder Method
Start with smaller podcasts (easier to book, good practice) and work up to larger ones. Each appearance gives you clips and experience to reference when pitching bigger shows.

### The Reciprocity Method
Before you need media coverage, build relationships by being useful to journalists: share their work, provide expert quotes for their stories, connect them with sources. When your book launches, you're not a stranger asking for a favor.

### The Excerpt Strategy
Offer exclusive excerpts to different outlets — a different chapter or scene for each. Exclusivity increases the chance they'll publish it, and multiple excerpts across outlets create broader coverage.

- A review solicitation plan with timeline
- A system for tracking pitches and responses

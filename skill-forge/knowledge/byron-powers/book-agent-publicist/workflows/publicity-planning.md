# Publicity Planning

## Overview

Publicity doesn't happen by accident. A book launch without a publicity plan is a book that relies entirely on the publisher's efforts — which, for most titles, means minimal support. This phase builds a structured publicity strategy covering pre-launch, launch week, and post-launch, tailored to your book's genre, audience, and resources.

## Workflow: Building Your Publicity Plan

### Step 1: Assess Your Starting Position

Before planning tactics, understand what you're working with:

**Publisher publicity support:**
- Does your publisher have an assigned publicist for your book?
- What's their planned publicity effort? (Ask your editor or agent)
- What materials will they provide? (ARCs, press releases, media lists)
- What's their timeline?

**Your own resources:**
- Existing platform size (newsletter subscribers, social media followers, website traffic)
- Media contacts or relationships
- Speaking experience and connections
- Budget for independent publicity efforts (hiring a publicist, paid promotion, events)
- Time available for publicity work

**Reality check:** For most debut and midlist authors, the publisher provides basic support (ARCs, catalog listing, some media outreach) and the author does the rest. Plan accordingly.

### Step 2: Define Your Publicity Goals

Set specific, measurable goals:

```markdown
# Publicity Goals: {Book Title}

## Awareness Goals
- {X} media placements (reviews, interviews, features)
- {X} podcast appearances
- {X} newsletter features or mentions
- {X} social media impressions during launch month

## Sales Goals
- {X} copies sold in first week
- {X} copies sold in first month
- Bestseller list target: {specific list, if applicable}

## Platform Goals
- {X} new newsletter subscribers
- {X} new social media followers
- {X} new website visitors

## Relationship Goals
- {X} new media contacts established
- {X} bookstore relationships built
- {X} author community connections made
```

### Step 3: Build the Timeline

**6 months before publication:**
- Finalize publicity goals
- Hire an independent publicist (if budget allows)
- Begin building media contact list
- Plan launch events
- Set up or refresh author website
- Prepare author photos (professional quality)

**4 months before publication:**
- ARCs (Advance Reader Copies) distributed to reviewers, media, and influencers
- Pitch long-lead media (magazines, major features — they work 3-4 months ahead)
- Confirm launch event venues and dates
- Begin social media pre-launch content

**2 months before publication:**
- Pitch short-lead media (newspapers, online outlets, podcasts)
- Confirm all launch week events and appearances
- Prepare social media launch content calendar
- Send ARCs to BookTok/Bookstagram/BookTube influencers
- Coordinate with publisher's publicity team on timing

**1 month before publication:**
- Follow up on all outstanding media pitches
- Finalize launch week schedule
- Prepare newsletter announcement
- Coordinate with bookstores for displays and events
- Brief your personal network (friends, family, colleagues who want to help)

**Launch week:**
- Execute all planned activities (see launch-execution)
- Monitor media coverage and social media mentions
- Respond to every review, interview, and mention
- Post daily on social media
- Send thank-you notes to everyone who helped

**Post-launch (months 1-6):**
- Continue pitching media (books have a longer publicity window than most products)
- Pursue speaking engagements and festival appearances
- Maintain social media presence around the book
- Track sales and adjust strategy
- Build toward paperback launch (if applicable)

### Step 4: Identify Your Channels

Not all publicity channels work for all books. Match channels to your genre and audience:

**Fiction channels:**

| Channel | Best For | Effort Level |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Book reviews (trade) | Literary fiction, genre fiction | Low (publisher handles) |
| Book reviews (consumer) | All fiction | Medium |
| Author interviews (print/online) | Literary fiction, high-concept | Medium |
| Podcast interviews | All fiction, especially genre | Medium |
| BookTok / Bookstagram | Romance, fantasy, thriller, YA | High |
| Book festivals | Literary fiction, established authors | Medium |
| Bookstore events | All fiction, especially local | Medium |
| Book clubs | Book club fiction, literary fiction | Low-Medium |
| Library events | All fiction | Low |

**Nonfiction channels:**

| Channel | Best For | Effort Level |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| Trade media (industry publications) | All nonfiction | Medium |
| Subject-matter media | Topic-specific nonfiction | High |
| Podcast interviews | All nonfiction | Medium |
| Conference speaking | Professional/technical nonfiction | High |
| Corporate/bulk sales | Business, professional development | Medium |
| University adoption | Academic, textbook-adjacent | Medium |
| LinkedIn / professional networks | Business, career, leadership | Medium |
| YouTube / online courses | How-to, technical | High |

### Step 5: Budget Planning

**If you have no budget:**
- Focus on free channels: social media, newsletter, personal network, podcast pitching
- Leverage your publisher's resources fully
- Trade favors with other authors (cross-promotion, newsletter swaps)
- Apply for festival and conference speaking slots (many cover travel)

**If you have a modest budget ($1,000-$5,000):**
- Professional author photos ($300-$800)
- Website refresh ($500-$1,500)
- Targeted social media advertising ($500-$2,000)
- Launch event costs (venue, refreshments)
- BookTok/Bookstagram gifting (sending copies to influencers)

**If you have a significant budget ($5,000-$20,000+):**
- Independent book publicist ($3,000-$10,000 for a 3-month campaign)
- Professional social media content creation
- Paid advertising (social media, Amazon, BookBub)
- Multi-city book tour
- Video content production

## Techniques

### The Angle Inventory Method
List 10 different angles a journalist could use to write about your book. Not just "author publishes novel" — think about timely hooks, personal stories, trend connections, local angles, and expert perspectives. The more angles you have, the more pitches you can customize.

### The Reverse Calendar Method
Start from publication date and work backward, placing each publicity task on the calendar. This reveals bottlenecks and ensures nothing falls through the cracks.

### The Concentric Circles Method
Start publicity outreach with your closest connections (personal network, existing followers) and expand outward (local media, then regional, then national). Each circle amplifies the next.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A complete publicity plan with timeline, channels, and budget
- Specific, measurable publicity goals
- A media contact list (or a plan to build one)
- A launch event plan
- Clarity on what your publisher will handle vs. what you'll handle yourself

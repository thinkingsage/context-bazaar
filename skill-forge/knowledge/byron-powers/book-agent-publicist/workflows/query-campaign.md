# Query Campaign

## Overview

Querying is a campaign, not a single event. Sending all your queries at once wastes your best material on an untested pitch. This phase structures the querying process into disciplined batches with built-in feedback loops, so you can refine your approach based on real market response.

## Workflow: Running the Campaign

### Step 1: Prepare Your Materials

Before sending a single query, have everything ready:

**Required materials:**
- Query letter (polished, 250-400 words)
- Synopsis — short (1 page) and standard (2-3 pages)
- Sample pages (first 5, 10, 25, and 50 pages — different agents request different amounts)
- Author bio paragraph
- Manuscript — complete, revised, and proofread

**Formatting checklist:**
- Query letter: plain text in the email body (not an attachment) unless guidelines say otherwise
- Synopsis: standard manuscript format (12pt, double-spaced, 1-inch margins)
- Sample pages: standard manuscript format, starting from page 1 of the manuscript
- Full manuscript: standard manuscript format, ready to send within 24 hours of a request

### Step 2: Set Up Tracking

Create a submission tracker before you send anything:

```markdown
# Query Submission Tracker

| # | Agent | Agency | Method | Materials Sent | Date Sent | Response | Response Date | Notes |

| 2 | {name} | {agency} | Email | Query + synopsis | {date} | — | — | |

## Response Summary

```

### Step 3: Execute in Batches

**Batch 1: The Test Batch (8-10 queries)**

Send to your Tier 1 agents first. This batch tests whether your query letter works.

- Customize each query with the personalization angle from your agent research
- Follow each agent's specific submission guidelines exactly
- Record the date sent and materials included

**Wait 4-6 weeks, then evaluate:**

| Result Pattern | What It Means | Action |
|---------------|---------------|--------|
| 0 requests out of 8-10 | Query letter isn't working | Revise the query before sending more |
| 1-2 requests out of 8-10 | Query is marginal | Revise and test with Batch 2 |
| 3+ requests out of 8-10 | Query is working | Proceed to Batch 2 with same query |
| Requests but rejections on manuscript | Query works, manuscript needs work | Consider revision before continuing |

**Batch 2-5: Iteration Batches (8-10 queries each)**

- Apply lessons from previous batches
- Move through Tier 2 and Tier 3 agents
- Continue tracking and evaluating response patterns
- Adjust the query letter if response rates drop

### Step 4: Manage Responses

**Request for partial manuscript (typically 50-100 pages):**
- Send within 24-48 hours
- Format per the agent's specifications
- Brief, professional cover email: "Thank you for your interest in [TITLE]. Please find the requested pages attached."
- Log the date and follow up mentally at 6-8 weeks

**Request for full manuscript:**
- Send within 24-48 hours
- Same professional cover email
- This is a strong signal of interest — but not a guarantee
- Log the date and follow up mentally at 8-12 weeks

**Personalized rejection:**
- Note any specific feedback — this is valuable
- If multiple agents give similar feedback, take it seriously
- A personalized rejection means the agent read carefully and saw potential
- Do not respond unless the agent explicitly invites resubmission

**Form rejection:**
- Log it and move on
- No response needed
- Don't take it personally — agents reject 95%+ of queries

**No response (after stated response window):**
- Check the agent's policy — many operate on "no response means no"
- If their policy allows nudges, send a brief follow-up after their stated response time plus 2 weeks
- If no policy stated, one polite nudge at 8 weeks is acceptable

### Step 5: The Nudge Protocol

When to nudge and how:

**Nudge on a query (no response after 8+ weeks):**
```
Subject: Re: Query — {TITLE}

Dear {Agent},

I wanted to follow up on my query for {TITLE}, a {word count} {genre}, sent on {date}. I understand you're busy and wanted to confirm it was received.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Best regards,
{Your name}
```

**Nudge on a requested partial or full (no response after 10-12 weeks):**
```
Subject: Re: Requested {partial/full} — {TITLE}

Dear {Agent},

I'm following up on the {partial/full manuscript} for {TITLE} that I sent on {date} at your request. I wanted to check in on its status.

Thank you for your continued interest.

Best regards,
{Your name}
```

**Notification of an offer (when another agent offers representation):**
```
Subject: Offer of Representation — {TITLE}

Dear {Agent},

I'm writing to let you know that I've received an offer of representation for {TITLE}. As you currently have my {query/partial/full}, I wanted to give you the opportunity to respond before I make my decision. I plan to make a decision by {date, typically 2 weeks out}.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Best regards,
{Your name}
```

### Step 6: Know When to Pause

**Pause and reassess if:**
- You've queried 30+ agents with zero requests → The query letter or premise may need fundamental revision
- You're getting requests but consistent rejections on the manuscript → The manuscript needs revision, not more queries
- You've exhausted your list → Consider whether the manuscript is ready, or whether the market timing is wrong

**Pausing is not failing.** Many successfully published novels went through multiple revision cycles between query rounds.

## Techniques

### The A/B Test Method
If you're unsure about your query, write two versions with different hooks. Send version A to half of Batch 1 and version B to the other half. Compare request rates.

### The Genre Peer Method
Find other writers querying in your genre (writing communities, Twitter, forums). Compare notes on which agents are responding and what's working. This is market intelligence.

### The Rejection Pattern Method
Track not just whether you're rejected, but how. Form rejections at the query stage = query problem. Personalized rejections at the query stage = close but not quite. Rejections after reading pages = manuscript problem. The pattern tells you where to focus revision.

### The Seasonal Awareness Method
Agent response times vary by season. January and September tend to be active query-reading months. Summer and December are slower. Don't panic about slow responses during industry quiet periods.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- A complete submission tracker with all queries logged
- Response data for at least your first 2-3 batches
- A revised query letter (if initial results warranted revision)
- Clear understanding of your response patterns and what they mean
- Either an offer of representation or a data-informed plan for next steps

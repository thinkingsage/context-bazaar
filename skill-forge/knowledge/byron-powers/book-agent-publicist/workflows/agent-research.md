# Agent Research

## Overview

Finding the right literary agent is matchmaking, not mass mailing. The goal isn't to query every agent who accepts your genre — it's to build a ranked list of agents whose taste, track record, and career vision align with your work. This phase turns the overwhelming universe of literary agents into a focused, strategic query list.

## Workflow: Building Your Agent List

### Step 1: Define Your Ideal Agent Profile

Before you research anyone, clarify what you're looking for:

**Genre fit:**
- Does the agent actively represent your genre and subgenre?
- Have they sold books similar to yours in the last 2-3 years?
- Do they represent the specific niche you're writing in, or just the broad category?

**Career alignment:**
- Are they building careers or doing one-off deals?
- Do they represent debut authors, or primarily established writers?
- What's their approach to editorial feedback before submission?
- Do they handle subsidiary rights (film, audio, foreign) in-house or through co-agents?

**Working style:**
- How communicative are they? (Some agents are hands-on; others are hands-off between deals)
- Do they provide editorial guidance, or expect submission-ready manuscripts?
- What's their typical response time?

### Step 2: Research Sources

**Primary sources:**

| Source | What It Tells You | Cost |
|--------|-------------------|------|
| Publishers Marketplace | Recent deals, deal sizes, genre focus | Subscription (~$25/month) |
| QueryTracker | Agent profiles, response times, success rates | Free + premium tier |
| Manuscript Wish List (#MSWL) | What agents are actively seeking | Free |
| Agent websites/blogs | Submission guidelines, client lists, taste | Free |
| Publisher's Marketplace Lunch | Industry news, deal announcements | Free daily email |
| Literary Rambles | Agent spotlights, interview roundups | Free |

**Secondary sources:**
- Agent interviews on podcasts and YouTube
- Conference panel appearances and recordings
- Social media presence (Twitter/X, Instagram, Bluesky)
- Acknowledgments pages in books you admire
- "Clients" or "Books" pages on agency websites

### Step 3: Build the Research Profile

For each agent you're considering, document:

```markdown
# Agent Profile: {Agent Name}

Agency: {Agency Name}
Website: {URL}
Submission guidelines: {URL}

## Genre Fit
- Genres represented: {list}
- Recent sales in my genre: {titles, years}
- MSWL mentions relevant to my book: {if any}

## Track Record
- Notable clients: {names}
- Recent deals: {titles, publishers, deal level if known}
- Years in the industry: {approximate}
- Agency reputation: {established, boutique, new but promising}

## Working Style
- Editorial approach: {editorial / hands-off / unknown}
- Communication style: {from interviews or client testimonials}
- Response time reputation: {fast / average / slow / no response means no}

## Personalization Angle
- Why this agent specifically: {what connects your book to their taste}
- Personalization hook for query: {specific book they repped, interview quote, MSWL match}

## Fit Score: {A / B / C}
- A = Strong match on genre, taste, and career vision
- B = Good match on genre, partial match on other factors
- C = Acceptable match, worth querying but not top priority
```

### Step 4: Rank and Organize

Sort your agents into tiers:

**Tier 1 (8-10 agents):** A-rated agents. Your dream list. These go in your first query batch.

**Tier 2 (10-15 agents):** B-rated agents. Strong matches with slightly less alignment. Second and third batches.

**Tier 3 (10-15 agents):** C-rated agents. Acceptable matches. Later batches or backup.

**Total target: 30-50 agents.** If you can't find 30 agents who represent your genre, your genre classification may be too narrow — revisit it.

### Step 5: Verify Before Querying

Before adding any agent to your final list, verify:

- **They're currently open to queries.** Check their website and social media for "closed to queries" notices.
- **They're legitimate.** Legitimate agents don't charge reading fees. Check Predators & Editors, Writer Beware, and Absolute Write forums.
- **They're at a reputable agency.** Check the agency's track record, not just the individual agent's.
- **Their submission guidelines are current.** Guidelines change. Always check the agent's website immediately before querying.
- **They haven't recently left their agency.** Agent moves are common. Verify they're still where you think they are.

## Red Flags

Watch for these warning signs:

- **Reading fees or "evaluation" fees.** Legitimate agents never charge to read your work.
- **Referrals to paid editing services.** Especially if the agent has a financial relationship with the editor.
- **No verifiable sales.** An agent who can't point to books they've sold to recognized publishers.
- **Vague or missing client lists.** Reputable agents are proud of their clients.
- **Pressure to sign quickly.** A legitimate agent will give you time to make a decision.
- **Upfront costs for marketing, website design, or "submission packages."** These are not standard agent services.

## Techniques

### The Reverse-Engineering Method
Find 10 books published in the last 3 years that are most similar to yours. Look up who represented them (acknowledgments pages, Publishers Marketplace, agent websites). Those agents are your highest-probability matches.

### The Conference Shortcut
Attend writing conferences (in person or virtual) where agents participate in pitch sessions or panels. Hearing an agent talk about what they love gives you better insight than any database entry.

### The Client Testimonial Method
Read interviews with authors represented by agents on your list. How do they describe the working relationship? This reveals the agent's style better than the agent's own marketing.

### The Deal Analysis Method
On Publishers Marketplace, look at an agent's recent deals. Note the publishers, deal levels, and genres. This tells you where they have relationships and what kind of advances they negotiate.

## Deliverables

By the end of this phase, you should have:
- Agent profiles for 30-50 researched agents
- A tiered ranking (Tier 1, 2, 3) based on fit
- Personalization angles for each agent
- Verified submission guidelines for your first batch
- Confidence that every agent on your list is legitimate and appropriate

## Connection to Other Phases

- **Query Campaign** — Your ranked list feeds directly into the batching strategy
- **Agent Evaluation** — Your research profiles become the basis for evaluating offers
- **Submission to Publishers** — Understanding an agent's publisher relationships helps you evaluate their submission strategy later
